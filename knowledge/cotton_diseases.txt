DISEASE 1: APHIDS
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
Aphids are tiny soft-bodied insects that attack cotton plants by sucking the juice
(sap) out of the plant. They are one of the most common and damaging pests in
cotton farming. These insects are very small – about 1 to 3 mm in size – and are
usually green, yellow, or black in color. They reproduce very quickly, which means
a small group of aphids can become thousands within just a few days. Aphids damage
the plant directly by feeding on it and also indirectly by spreading plant viruses
from one plant to another. Their feeding weakens the plant, reduces growth, and can
cause serious loss in cotton yield if not controlled on time.

2. SYMPTOMS
-----------
- Leaves curl downward or become wrinkled and twisted.
- Small clusters of green, yellow, or black insects visible on the underside of
  leaves, on young shoots, and on buds.
- Sticky, shiny liquid called "honeydew" is found on leaves. This is left behind by
  aphids after feeding.
- A black sooty mold (fungus) grows on the honeydew, making leaves look dirty and
  black.
- Leaves turn yellow and may drop early.
- Young plants show stunted growth (plants look shorter and weaker than normal).
- In heavy attack, flower buds and bolls may fall off the plant.
- Ants are often seen moving around aphid colonies because ants feed on honeydew.

3. CAUSES
---------
- Aphids are insects (pests) that come from outside the field or survive in nearby
  weeds and plants.
- They spread from plant to plant by flying or by wind.
- They also travel to new fields on farming tools, clothing, and even on ants.
- Aphids can spread cotton leaf curl virus (CLCuV), which causes even more damage.

4. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
-----------------------
- Aphids multiply very fast in warm and dry weather (temperature between 20°C
  to 30°C).
- Mild winters and early warm summers increase aphid populations.
- Excessive use of nitrogen fertilizers makes plants grow soft and tender, which
  attracts more aphids.
- Lack of natural enemies (like ladybird beetles) in the field allows aphids to
  increase unchecked.
- Monoculture farming (growing only cotton year after year) increases aphid risk.

5. PREVENTION
-------------
- Regularly inspect your field, especially the underside of leaves, every 3–4 days.
- Avoid using too much nitrogen fertilizer, as it promotes soft leafy growth that
  attracts aphids.
- Plant cotton on time (avoid very late sowing) to reduce aphid pressure.
- Maintain proper spacing between plants for better airflow.
- Remove and destroy weeds in and around the field, as weeds host aphids.
- Use resistant cotton varieties where available in your region.
- Grow border crops like maize or sorghum around the cotton field to attract
  beneficial insects.

6. TREATMENT / CONTROL
-----------------------
CULTURAL PRACTICES:
- Remove and destroy heavily infested leaves and shoots.
- Spray a strong jet of water to wash aphids off plants in small fields.
- Deep plough the field after harvest to destroy eggs and pupae in the soil.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Encourage natural predators: Ladybird beetles (ladybugs), lacewings, and
  parasitic wasps feed on aphids and keep their numbers low.
- Release Chrysoperla carnea (green lacewing) eggs or larvae in the field
  (available from agricultural offices or vendors).
- Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides that kill beneficial insects.

RECOMMENDED PESTICIDES (use only when necessary):
- Imidacloprid 17.8% SL: Spray 100–125 ml per acre, mixed in water.
- Thiamethoxam 25% WG: Spray 40 grams per acre.
- Dimethoate 30% EC: Spray 200–300 ml per acre.
- Neem-based pesticide (Azadirachtin 0.03% EC): Spray 400–600 ml per acre.
  (Neem spray is safe, eco-friendly, and does not harm beneficial insects.)
NOTE: Always follow the label instructions on pesticide packets. Wear gloves and a
mask when spraying. Do not spray near water sources or ponds.

7. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Scout the field every week. Start treatment only when you see more than 10–15
  aphids per leaf or per plant (called the Economic Threshold Level or ETL).
- First try neem-based or biological sprays before using chemical pesticides.
- Alternate between different pesticide groups to prevent aphids from becoming
  resistant to chemicals.
- Set up yellow sticky traps in the field to monitor and catch flying aphids.
  (Use 10–15 traps per acre.)
- Grow flowering plants like marigold at the border of the field to attract
  beneficial insects that eat aphids.

8. FARMER TIPS
--------------
- Walk through your field early in the morning when it is cool – aphids are easier
  to spot on the underside of leaves at that time.
- Do not wait until the attack is severe. Start treatment early.
- If you see ants climbing your cotton plants, check for aphids – ants and aphids
  are often found together.
- Neem oil spray (5 ml neem oil + 1 liter water + a few drops of soap) is a cheap
  and effective home remedy for mild aphid attacks.
- Keep records of when aphids appeared in previous years – this helps you be
  prepared next season.

DISEASE 2: ARMY WORM
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
Army worm is the name given to the caterpillar (larva) of certain moth species,
most commonly the Spodoptera species. They are called "army worms" because they
move through fields in large groups, like an army, eating everything in their path.
In cotton, the most common types are Spodoptera exigua (beet army worm) and
Spodoptera frugiperda (fall army worm). The adult is a moth that lays eggs on the
cotton plant. After hatching, the caterpillars begin feeding heavily on leaves, buds,
and bolls. Army worm attacks can destroy large portions of a cotton crop in a very
short time if not controlled quickly. They are active mostly at night and hide
during the day under leaves or in the soil near the base of the plant.

2. SYMPTOMS
-----------
- Irregular holes or ragged edges on leaves where caterpillars have been feeding.
- In young plants, complete stripping of leaves may occur (plant looks bare).
- "Window paning" – young caterpillars eat only the soft green part of the leaf,
  leaving a thin transparent skin that looks like a window.
- Presence of caterpillars (green, brown, or striped) on leaves, on soil near the
  base of plants, or inside bolls.
- Green or black pellet-shaped droppings (frass) of caterpillars seen on leaves and
  on the ground below the plant.
- Flowers and small bolls may be chewed or damaged.
- Large caterpillars may enter cotton bolls and feed on the fiber inside.
- Egg masses (tiny round eggs in clusters, sometimes covered with hairs) on the
  upper surface of leaves.

3. CAUSES
---------
- Army worm damage is caused by the larva (caterpillar) stage of moths belonging
  to the Spodoptera group.
- The adult moth lays eggs in groups on cotton leaves at night.
- After 2–3 days, eggs hatch and the caterpillars start feeding.
- Caterpillars go through 5–6 stages (instars) over 2–3 weeks before becoming pupae
  and then adult moths.
- Adult moths fly to new fields and continue the cycle.

4. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
-----------------------
- Warm and humid weather (temperature 25°C–35°C, humidity above 60%) is ideal for
  army worm multiplication.
- Heavy rains followed by dry, warm periods encourage outbreaks.
- Dense plant canopy (overcrowded plants) provides shelter for caterpillars.
- Late-planted cotton is often at higher risk.
- Fields near forests, grasslands, or fallows are more likely to be invaded.
- High nitrogen use promotes leafy growth that attracts egg-laying moths.

5. PREVENTION
-------------
- Sow cotton at the recommended time for your region to avoid peak moth activity.
- Practice crop rotation – do not plant cotton in the same field every year.
- Deep plough the field before and after the season to expose pupae in the soil to
  sunlight and birds.
- Maintain proper plant spacing to reduce canopy density.
- Keep field borders free of weeds that can harbor moths.
- Destroy crop residues after harvest.

6. TREATMENT / CONTROL
-----------------------
CULTURAL PRACTICES:
- Hand-pick caterpillars and egg masses from small fields and destroy them by
  drowning in kerosene water or burying.
- Use light traps or pheromone traps to catch adult moths before they lay eggs.
- Till the soil between rows to expose and kill pupae.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Spray Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) – a natural bacterial pesticide that kills
  caterpillars without harming people, animals, or beneficial insects.
  Use Bt at 500 grams per acre mixed in water. Best results on young caterpillars.
- Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus (NPV) – a virus spray specifically effective against
  Spodoptera species. Available from some agricultural departments.
- Encourage natural predators: parasitic wasps (Trichogramma), ground beetles,
  birds, and spiders help control army worm populations.

RECOMMENDED PESTICIDES:
- Chlorpyrifos 20% EC: Spray 400–500 ml per acre.
- Emamectin Benzoate 5% SG: Spray 80–100 grams per acre.
- Spinosad 45% SC: Spray 60–75 ml per acre (also eco-friendly, less harmful to
  beneficial insects).
- Lambda-cyhalothrin 5% EC: Spray 200 ml per acre.
NOTE: Spray in the evening or early morning when caterpillars are active. Target
the underside of leaves for better results.

7. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Install pheromone traps (Spodoptera-specific lures) at the rate of 5 traps per
  acre to monitor and trap male moths.
- Scout the field twice a week, especially in the early morning.
- Start chemical treatment only when you find more than 5 caterpillars per plant.
- Use Bt sprays first, then shift to chemical pesticides only if the attack is
  heavy.
- Attract birds (like mynahs and sparrows) by keeping perches in the field – birds
  eat caterpillars.

8. FARMER TIPS
--------------
- Check your field every evening, as army worms are most active after sunset.
- Look for the zig-zag pattern of feeding damage on leaves – this is a classic
  sign of army worm.
- Young caterpillars (first and second stage) are easiest to kill with sprays.
  Do not wait until they become large.
- If you see moths flying around light sources at night, expect egg-laying in your
  field within 2–3 days. Start monitoring immediately.
- Share information with your neighboring farmers – army worms move from field to
  field, so coordinated spraying gives better results.

DISEASE 3: BACTERIAL BLIGHT
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
Bacterial Blight is one of the most serious diseases of cotton caused by a
bacterium called Xanthomonas citri subsp. malvacearum (also known as Xanthomonas
axonopodis pv. malvacearum). This disease is also called "Angular Leaf Spot" or
"Cotton Bacteriosis." It can infect the plant at any stage of growth – from
seedling to mature plant. The bacteria enter the plant through small openings
(stomata), wounds, or cuts on the plant. Once inside, they destroy plant cells and
block the vessels that carry water and nutrients. Bacterial blight can reduce cotton
yield by 20–60% in a bad year and also reduces the quality of cotton fiber. It
spreads very fast in wet and warm weather conditions.

2. SYMPTOMS
-----------
ON LEAVES:
- Water-soaked (dark, wet-looking) small spots first appear on leaves.
- These spots dry out and turn brown with a yellow border around them.
- The spots have angular (square or irregular) edges because they are limited by
  the veins of the leaf – this is why it is called "Angular Leaf Spot."
- Severely infected leaves turn yellow and fall off the plant.
- Black streaks may appear along the leaf veins.

ON STEMS AND BRANCHES:
- Dark brown to black water-soaked streaks appear on stems and young branches.
- Infected branches may die and turn black (this is called "black arm").
- The stem may crack and dry out at infected areas.

ON BOLLS:
- Small, circular, water-soaked spots appear on the surface of bolls.
- These spots enlarge and turn dark brown or black.
- Infected bolls may not open properly, and the lint (fiber) inside can be stained
  and damaged.

ON SEEDLINGS:
- Young seedlings show "damping off" – they fall over and die near the soil surface.
- Cotyledons (seed leaves) develop angular, dark spots.

3. CAUSES
---------
- The disease is caused by the bacterium Xanthomonas citri subsp. malvacearum.
- The bacteria survive in infected crop residues, seeds, and soil between seasons.
- They spread through rain, wind, irrigation water, insects, farm tools, and hands.
- Infected seeds are the primary way the disease enters a new field.

4. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
-----------------------
- Wet and humid weather (humidity above 70%) with frequent rains is ideal for
  bacterial blight spread.
- Warm temperature between 25°C to 35°C speeds up bacterial growth.
- Heavy rains, strong winds, and hailstorms create wounds on plants, which allow
  bacteria to enter easily.
- Dense planting and poor air circulation create moisture that helps bacteria spread.
- Late rains during the boll formation stage cause severe boll infection.

5. PREVENTION
-------------
- Always use certified, disease-free cotton seeds.
- Use resistant cotton varieties recommended for your area.
- Treat seeds with hot water (52°C for 30 minutes) before planting to kill bacteria
  on the seed surface.
- Practice crop rotation – avoid growing cotton in the same field for 2 years in a
  row.
- Maintain proper plant spacing to allow good air circulation.
- Do not use irrigation water from fields where bacterial blight has been seen.
- Remove and destroy infected plant parts immediately.
- Disinfect farm tools with bleach solution or wash them before moving between
  fields.

6. TREATMENT / CONTROL
-----------------------
CULTURAL PRACTICES:
- Remove and burn infected leaves, branches, and bolls as soon as symptoms are seen.
- Avoid working in the field when plants are wet from rain or dew, as this helps
  spread the bacteria.
- Drain waterlogged areas in the field quickly after heavy rains.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Spray with Trichoderma viride or Pseudomonas fluorescens (bio-fungicide) mixed in
  water to improve plant immunity.

RECOMMENDED TREATMENTS:
- Copper-based bactericides:
  Copper Oxychloride 50% WP: Spray 600–800 grams per acre mixed in water.
  Bordeaux Mixture (1%): Prepare by mixing 1 kg copper sulfate + 1 kg lime in
  100 liters of water. Spray on plants.
- Streptomycin Sulfate 90% + Tetracycline 10% (combination): Spray 15–20 grams
  per acre. (Use only when advised by an agricultural officer – do not overuse
  antibiotics.)
- Seed treatment: Soak seeds in Streptocycline (100 ppm) solution for 30 minutes
  before planting.

7. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Scout the field every week from seedling stage onwards.
- Use disease forecasting: if heavy rain is expected, apply a preventive copper
  spray 2–3 days before or immediately after rain.
- Destroy crop residues after harvest by deep ploughing or burning, as this kills
  bacteria in the soil and on plant material.
- Grow a non-host crop (like wheat, sorghum, or chickpea) after cotton to break the
  disease cycle.

8. FARMER TIPS
--------------
- Never buy seeds from unknown sources. Always buy certified seeds with a seal
  of quality.
- After handling infected plants, wash your hands and tools before touching healthy
  plants.
- If you had bacterial blight last season, do not use saved seeds from those plants.
- Copper spray is your best protection. Apply it as a preventive measure before
  the rainy season.
- If you see dark streaks on stems (black arm), act immediately – do not wait.
  Remove infected branches and spray copper bactericide the same day.

DISEASE 4: POWDERY MILDEW
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
Powdery Mildew is a fungal disease of cotton caused by the fungus Leveillula taurica
(also sometimes associated with other fungal species). It is easy to identify
because it creates a white or grey powdery coating on plant surfaces. Unlike most
fungal diseases that need wet and rainy conditions, Powdery Mildew actually thrives
in dry and warm conditions with moderate humidity. This makes it a common problem
in late-season cotton in many regions of India. The disease mainly affects leaves
but can also infect stems, buds, and young bolls. It reduces photosynthesis (the
plant's ability to make food from sunlight), which weakens the plant and reduces
yield and fiber quality.

2. SYMPTOMS
-----------
ON LEAVES:
- White to grey powdery patches (like someone sprinkled flour) appear on the upper
  and lower surface of leaves.
- The powdery patches start as small spots and expand to cover the entire leaf
  surface.
- Infected leaves may turn yellow and then brown before falling off the plant.
- In some cases, the upper surface shows yellow patches while the white powder is
  more visible on the underside of the leaf.

ON STEMS AND SHOOTS:
- White powdery coating can be seen on young, tender stems and growing tips.
- Infected stems may become stunted and twisted.

ON FLOWERS AND BOLLS:
- In heavy infections, flowers and young bolls may show powdery coating.
- Infected bolls may not open fully and fiber quality is reduced.

GENERAL:
- In severe cases, the entire plant looks greyish-white from a distance.
- Plant growth slows down noticeably.

3. CAUSES
---------
- Powdery Mildew is caused by the fungus Leveillula taurica.
- The fungus produces millions of tiny spores (seeds) that spread through the air
  with the wind.
- The spores land on plant surfaces and grow into the plant.
- The fungus lives on the outer surface of the plant and sends feeding tubes inside
  the leaf.
- It survives between seasons on infected plant debris and on other host plants
  such as tomato, brinjal, and various weeds.

4. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
-----------------------
- Unlike most fungi, Powdery Mildew spreads best in DRY conditions with moderate
  humidity (40–70%).
- Temperature between 25°C to 30°C is most favorable.
- Cool nights followed by warm, dry days help the fungus grow.
- Dense planting with poor air circulation increases infection.
- Powdery mildew is most common in the late season (October–November in many Indian
  growing zones).
- Shaded areas in the field often show more infection.

5. PREVENTION
-------------
- Use resistant cotton varieties recommended for your region.
- Avoid very dense planting – maintain proper row and plant spacing for good airflow.
- Remove and destroy infected plant parts promptly.
- Avoid excessive irrigation, especially near the end of the growing season.
- Clean up all crop residues and weeds after harvest to remove the fungus's resting
  sites.
- Practice crop rotation with non-host crops.
- Avoid planting cotton near tomato, brinjal, or pepper fields, as these can be
  sources of the fungus.

6. TREATMENT / CONTROL
-----------------------
CULTURAL PRACTICES:
- Prune and remove heavily infected leaves and shoots.
- Ensure proper spacing between plants.
- Avoid overhead irrigation in the evening – wet leaves at night encourage many
  fungal diseases.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Spray with Trichoderma harzianum (bio-fungicide): 2.5 kg per acre mixed in water.
- Neem oil spray (0.5–1%): 5 ml neem oil per liter of water with a few drops of
  soap. Effective for mild to moderate infections.
- Cow urine spray (diluted 1:10 with water) is a traditional remedy that can
  reduce mild powdery mildew.

RECOMMENDED FUNGICIDES:
- Sulphur 80% WP (Wettable Sulphur): Spray 600–800 grams per acre. Very effective
  and affordable for Powdery Mildew.
- Hexaconazole 5% EC: Spray 200–300 ml per acre.
- Myclobutanil 10% WP: Spray 100–120 grams per acre.
- Dinocap 48% EC: Spray 200 ml per acre.
NOTE: Start fungicide sprays at the first sign of disease. Repeat every 10–15 days
if the disease persists.

7. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Monitor your field from the beginning of the cotton season and pay attention to
  late-season weather conditions.
- Apply preventive sulphur dust or spray before the late-season dry spell begins.
- Destroy all crop residues immediately after harvest.
- Intercropping cotton with less susceptible crops can reduce disease spread.
- Avoid water stress in plants – mild water stress can make plants more susceptible
  to Powdery Mildew.

8. FARMER TIPS
--------------
- Powdery Mildew looks like white flour on leaves. If you wipe it with your finger
  and it comes off as powder, it is very likely Powdery Mildew.
- Sulphur spray is the cheapest and most effective solution. Apply it early.
- Do not let the disease spread – remove infected leaves immediately and bury or
  burn them away from the field.
- Check the shaded and dense areas of your field first – Powdery Mildew often
  starts in areas with less sunlight and airflow.
- Apply sulphur in the morning on a clear day. Do not apply sulphur spray in very
  hot weather (above 35°C) as it can burn the leaves.

DISEASE 5: TARGET SPOT
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
Target Spot of cotton is a fungal disease caused by the fungus Corynespora
cassiicola. It gets its name from the appearance of the disease spots, which have
rings inside rings – like a shooting target or a bull's-eye. Target Spot is
increasingly important in many cotton-growing regions and can cause significant
yield loss by damaging leaves, causing early leaf drop, and reducing photosynthesis.
It affects leaves at all stages of growth, but is most damaging during the boll
formation and maturation stages. The disease can also infect bolls and stems in
severe cases. Early and widespread defoliation (loss of leaves) is the biggest
impact of this disease and can reduce yield by 15–40%.

2. SYMPTOMS
-----------
ON LEAVES:
- Small, circular to irregular brown spots appear on the leaf surface.
- As the spots grow, they develop concentric rings (ring inside ring), creating the
  "target" or "bull's-eye" appearance.
- Spots may be surrounded by a yellow halo (ring of yellow tissue).
- Multiple spots can merge together to form large brown patches.
- Severely infected leaves turn yellow and fall off the plant prematurely
  (early leaf drop).
- On the underside of the leaf, the spots may appear lighter in color.

ON BOLLS:
- Small, round, brown sunken spots appear on boll surface.
- Infected bolls may not open properly and the cotton fiber inside can be damaged
  or stained.

ON STEMS:
- Dark brown oval lesions may appear on young stems and leaf stalks (petioles).
- Infected stems may weaken and crack.

ON BRACTS (leaf-like structures around bolls):
- Brown lesions with target-like rings may appear on bracts, causing them to turn
  brown and dry out.

3. CAUSES
---------
- Target Spot is caused by the fungus Corynespora cassiicola.
- The fungus survives on infected plant residues in the soil between seasons.
- It spreads through spores that travel by wind, rain splash, and irrigation water.
- The spores land on plant surfaces and germinate in wet conditions.
- The fungus can also infect many other crops (tomato, cucumber, soybean) and weeds,
  allowing it to survive even when cotton is not in the field.

4. FAVORABLE CONDITIONS
-----------------------
- Hot and humid conditions (temperature 25°C–35°C, humidity above 80%) are ideal
  for the spread of Target Spot.
- Frequent rainfall, dew, and foggy mornings create moisture on leaves that allows
  fungal spores to germinate.
- The disease is most severe during the middle to late part of the cotton season
  when the canopy is dense.
- Dense planting and overcrowded fields trap moisture and increase infection.
- Extended periods of cloudy weather with reduced sunlight favor the disease.
- Fields with history of Target Spot and high levels of crop residue in the soil
  are at higher risk.

5. PREVENTION
-------------
- Use cotton varieties that are less susceptible to Target Spot where available.
- Maintain proper plant spacing to improve air circulation and reduce moisture in
  the canopy.
- Practice crop rotation with non-host crops like wheat, sorghum, or chickpea.
- Remove and destroy all crop residues and infected plant material after harvest.
- Deep plough the field after harvest to bury crop residues and destroy the fungus.
- Avoid excessive use of nitrogen fertilizer, as overly lush canopies trap moisture.
- Avoid overhead irrigation in the evening or at night.

6. TREATMENT / CONTROL
-----------------------
CULTURAL PRACTICES:
- Remove and dispose of heavily infected leaves, especially from the lower part of
  the plant where the disease usually starts.
- Improve ventilation in the field by wider row spacing.
- Reduce irrigation frequency during wet periods.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Spray Trichoderma viride or Trichoderma harzianum (bio-fungicide): helps protect
  plants when used as a preventive spray.
- Bacillus subtilis-based products can suppress the fungus on plant surfaces.

RECOMMENDED FUNGICIDES:
- Azoxystrobin 23% SC: Spray 200 ml per acre. Very effective against Target Spot.
- Pyraclostrobin + Boscalid: Spray as recommended on the label. Good broad-spectrum
  coverage for fungal diseases.
- Propiconazole 25% EC: Spray 200 ml per acre.
- Carbendazim 50% WP: Spray 250–300 grams per acre.
- Mancozeb 75% WP: Spray 600–800 grams per acre. Affordable and widely available.
NOTE: Begin spraying as soon as you notice first symptoms. Repeat spray after
14 days if conditions remain wet and humid.

7. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Scout the field every 7 days during the rainy and humid periods.
- Focus on the lower leaves first – Target Spot usually starts at the bottom of the
  plant and moves upward.
- Alternate between different types of fungicides to prevent the fungus from
  developing resistance.
- Apply preventive fungicide sprays during extended wet and cloudy weather, even
  before symptoms appear.
- Use narrow-spectrum biological sprays as a preventive measure during early season.
- Remove all volunteer cotton plants (cotton that has grown from last year's seeds)
  as they can serve as a source of the fungus.

8. FARMER TIPS
--------------
- Look for the "target" or "ring within ring" pattern on leaves – this is the most
  reliable way to identify Target Spot quickly.
- Start looking at the bottom leaves of the plant first. The disease moves upward.
- Do not delay treatment – once the lower canopy starts dropping leaves, yield loss
  has already begun.
- If the disease is in the field, avoid moving through it during wet weather, as
  you can carry spores on your clothing and spread the disease.
- After a week of rain, always check your field immediately for early signs of
  Target Spot.
- Use Mancozeb spray as a low-cost preventive treatment at the start of the wet
  season.

DISEASE 6: HEALTHY COTTON PLANT – REFERENCE GUIDE FOR FARMERS
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
-----------
This section describes what a healthy cotton plant looks like at different stages
of growth. Farmers should use this as a reference to compare with their actual
plants in the field. Knowing what a healthy plant looks like helps you to quickly
spot problems before they become serious.

2. APPEARANCE OF A HEALTHY PLANT
----------------------------------
SEEDLING STAGE (Days 1–21 after planting):
- Seedlings emerge evenly and stand upright.
- First leaves (cotyledons) are smooth, round, and bright green.
- Stem is firm, green, and upright – not bent or rotting at the base.
- Roots are white, firm, and well-branched when pulled out.
- Plants grow at a steady pace – about 2–4 cm per week.

VEGETATIVE STAGE (Weeks 3–8):
- Leaves are large, deep green, 3-lobed or 5-lobed, with a smooth or slightly
  rough surface.
- No spots, holes, yellow patches, or powdery coating on leaves.
- Stem is green, strong, and upright. No cracks or black streaks.
- Plant grows vigorously with new leaves appearing regularly.
- No curling, twisting, or cupping of leaves.

FLOWERING STAGE (Weeks 8–12):
- Flower buds (squares) are plump, firm, and pyramid-shaped.
- Flowers are creamy white on the first day and turn pink/red by the second day
  (this is completely normal).
- Petals fall naturally within 2–3 days after opening.
- Flowers are evenly distributed along branches.
- Leaves near flowers are large and healthy green.

BOLL DEVELOPMENT STAGE (Weeks 12–20):
- Bolls (fruit) are green, firm, and oval-shaped.
- Boll surface is smooth and free from spots, sunken areas, or cracks.
- Bolls develop evenly on the plant from the bottom to the top.
- Plant leaves remain green and attached to the plant.

BOLL OPENING AND MATURATION (Weeks 18–25 and beyond):
- Mature bolls split open naturally into 3–5 sections.
- Fiber (lint) inside is bright white, fluffy, and clean.
- Seed inside the fiber is dark and fully formed.
- Leaves gradually turn yellow and drop naturally as the plant matures.
  (This is normal at the very end of the season.)

3. HEALTHY LEAF CHARACTERISTICS
---------------------------------
- Color: Deep green (not pale yellow, not dark blackish).
- Surface: Smooth or slightly hairy, no powdery coating.
- Shape: Flat and fully expanded, not curled, rolled, or cupped.
- Edges: Normal leaf margins, not burnt or brown at edges.
- Underside: No insects, no white flies, no sticky honeydew.
- Size: Leaves grow steadily larger as the plant matures.

4. HEALTHY STEM AND ROOT CHARACTERISTICS
------------------------------------------
- Stem: Green to light brown at the base, firm and straight.
- No dark streaks, oozing liquid, or cracks on the stem.
- Branches grow at regular intervals along the main stem.
- Roots: White, firm, and branched. No rotting or dark coloration.
- Taproot goes deep into the soil.

5. HEALTHY PLANT GROWTH INDICATORS
-------------------------------------
- Plant height is appropriate for the variety and growth stage.
- Regular appearance of new leaves and branches.
- Buds, flowers, and bolls are present in good numbers.
- No unusual dropping of flower buds, flowers, or young bolls.
  (Note: Some early bud drop is normal, but excessive drop is a warning sign.)
- Color remains green throughout the vegetative and early boll stage.

6. SIGNS THAT INDICATE A PROBLEM (Early Warning Signs)
---------------------------------------------------------
If you see any of the following on your cotton plants, investigate immediately:
- Yellowing of leaves, especially younger leaves at the top.
- Leaves curling upward or downward.
- White powdery coating on leaves.
- Brown spots of any shape or size on leaves.
- Sticky, shiny substance on leaves (honeydew from insects).
- Tiny insects visible under the leaves or on growing tips.
- Flower buds dropping before they open.
- Bolls turning black or developing soft spots.
- Foul smell from the plant or soil near the plant.
- Plants wilting even when the soil has enough water.

7. FARMER TIPS FOR MAINTAINING HEALTHY PLANTS
-----------------------------------------------
- Walk through your field at least twice a week and observe plants carefully.
- Take a magnifying lens to check the underside of leaves for insects and mites.
- Compare a suspected sick plant with a healthy plant nearby.
- Take a photo of any unusual symptom and consult your agricultural extension
  officer or KrushiBandhu AI assistant.
- A healthy plant starts with healthy seeds. Always use certified, treated seeds.
- Follow your crop calendar for fertilizer, irrigation, and pest management
  activities – prevention is better than cure.
- Keep notes each year about what problems you faced and when – this helps you
  prepare better for the next season.
