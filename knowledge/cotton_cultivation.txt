SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION TO COTTON FARMING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton is one of the most important cash crops in India. It is called the
"White Gold" of Indian agriculture. India is one of the largest producers
and exporters of cotton in the world. Cotton provides raw material for the
textile industry and supports the livelihoods of millions of farmers,
workers, and traders across the country.

In India, cotton is grown in about 120–130 lakh hectares of land every year.
The major cotton-growing states are Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana, Andhra
Pradesh, Karnataka, Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, Punjab, and Haryana.

There are two main types of cotton grown in India:
  1. Desi cotton (G. arboreum and G. herbaceum) – shorter staple, traditional.
  2. American cotton (G. hirsutum) – most widely grown, longer staple, higher
     yield, includes Bt cotton varieties.
  3. Egyptian cotton (G. barbadense) – extra-long staple, grown in limited areas.

Today, most Indian farmers grow Bt cotton (Bollgard varieties) because it
provides built-in protection against bollworm pests and gives higher yields.
Cotton is a kharif crop – it is sown in summer and harvested in winter.

A good understanding of cotton cultivation from land preparation to harvest
is essential to maximize yield and profit. This guide covers all the key
steps in simple language.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: SUITABLE CLIMATE AND TEMPERATURE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton is a warm-weather crop. It needs a long, frost-free growing season
of 150–180 days. The following climate conditions are ideal:

  TEMPERATURE:
  - Germination (seed sprouting): 18–30°C. Best at 25–30°C.
  - Vegetative growth stage    : 25–35°C.
  - Flowering stage            : 25–30°C (cool nights improve boll set).
  - Boll development stage     : 27–32°C during the day.
  - Too hot (above 40°C)       : Causes flower and boll shedding.
  - Too cold (below 15°C)      : Slows growth and damages young plants.
  - Frost                      : Kills the cotton plant. Avoid frost-prone
                                 areas or harvest before frost arrives.

  RAINFALL:
  - Ideal annual rainfall: 50–100 cm, well distributed.
  - Cotton needs good moisture during germination, flowering, and boll
    filling stages.
  - Too much rain during boll opening causes lint staining and quality loss.
  - Dry, sunny weather during boll opening is preferred.

  SUNLIGHT:
  - Cotton needs 8–10 hours of bright sunlight per day.
  - Longer daylight hours promote vegetative growth; shorter days
    (after monsoon) promote flowering and fruiting.
  - Cloudy weather reduces photosynthesis and increases pest-disease risk.

  HUMIDITY:
  - Moderate humidity (50–70%) during growing season is ideal.
  - Very high humidity (above 80%) during boll stage encourages
    fungal diseases and boll rot.

SECTION 3: SOIL REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton can be grown in a wide range of soils but performs best under
specific soil conditions.

  SOIL TYPE:
  - Best soil: Deep, well-drained, medium to heavy black cotton soil
    (Vertisols / Regur soil). This soil holds moisture well and is
    naturally fertile.
  - Also suitable: Sandy loam, clay loam soils with good drainage.
  - Avoid: Waterlogged, saline, alkaline, or very light sandy soils.

  SOIL DEPTH:
  - Minimum 60 cm deep. Deeper is better (90–120 cm ideal) as cotton
    has a deep taproot system.
  - Shallow soils cause stunted growth and poor yield.

  SOIL pH:
  - Ideal range: 6.0–8.0 (slightly acidic to slightly alkaline).
  - Below pH 6.0: Nutrient deficiencies (especially phosphorus) occur.
  - Above pH 8.5: Micronutrient deficiencies (iron, zinc, manganese)
    and reduced germination.

  DRAINAGE:
  - Good drainage is essential. Cotton does not tolerate waterlogging.
  - Even one day of waterlogging at seedling stage can kill the crop.
  - If soil drainage is poor, form raised beds or ridges before sowing.

  ORGANIC MATTER:
  - Higher organic matter improves soil structure, water retention, and
    nutrient availability.
  - Add 4–5 tonnes per acre of well-decomposed FYM (Farm Yard Manure)
    or compost during land preparation.

  MICRONUTRIENTS:
  - Cotton frequently shows deficiency of Zinc (Zn), Boron (B), and
    Iron (Fe). Soil testing is strongly recommended before each season.
  - Apply Zinc Sulphate @ 10 kg per acre if soil test shows deficiency.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: LAND PREPARATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Good land preparation creates the ideal seedbed for cotton germination
and root development. This is a critical step that affects the entire crop.

  STEP 1 – DEEP PLOUGHING (Summer Ploughing):
  - Plough the field 20–30 cm deep using a disc plough or MB plough.
  - Do this in April–May (summer) after the previous crop is removed.
  - Benefits: Breaks hardpan, exposes pest pupae and disease spores to
    sunlight, improves water penetration, removes deep-rooted weeds.

  STEP 2 – HARROWING AND LEVELLING:
  - After deep ploughing, harrow the field 2–3 times to break clods.
  - Level the field properly for uniform water distribution.
  - Remove stones, crop residue, and stubble from the field.

  STEP 3 – ADDING ORGANIC MATTER:
  - Apply 4–5 tonnes per acre of well-decomposed FYM or compost.
  - Mix it evenly into the soil during the last harrowing.
  - This improves soil structure and adds nutrients slowly.

  STEP 4 – FORMING RIDGES AND FURROWS (if irrigated):
  - For irrigated cotton, form ridges and furrows at the required
    plant spacing (row spacing 90–120 cm).
  - This helps in proper irrigation and drainage management.

  STEP 5 – SOIL TESTING:
  - Collect soil samples before the season and get them tested at the
    nearest Soil Testing Laboratory.
  - Apply fertilizers based on the soil test report, not on guesswork.
  - Key nutrients to test: N, P, K, pH, EC, Organic Carbon, Zinc, Boron.

SECTION 5: SEED SELECTION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Choosing the right seed variety is one of the most important decisions in
cotton farming. The right variety can significantly increase yield and
reduce problems.

  TYPES OF COTTON SEEDS AVAILABLE IN INDIA:
  1. Bt Cotton Hybrid Seeds (Bollgard I / Bollgard II):
     - Most widely used in India (about 95% of area under Bt cotton).
     - Contains Bt genes (Cry1Ac and Cry2Ab in BG-II) that protect
       against American bollworm, Spotted bollworm, and Pink bollworm.
     - Higher yield potential: 15–25 quintals of seed cotton per acre.
     - Available from companies like Mahyco, Bayer, Rasi Seeds, Nuziveedu,
       Ankur, Ajeet, Tulasi, etc.
  2. Non-Bt Hybrid Seeds:
     - Used in some regions; require more pesticide use for bollworms.
  3. Desi Varieties (open-pollinated):
     - Used in certain specific regions; lower input cost; more suitable
       for organic farming.

  GUIDELINES FOR SEED SELECTION:
  - Always buy certified seeds from registered dealers or companies.
  - Look for the government certification tag on the packet.
  - Choose a variety recommended by your State Agriculture Department
    for your region and soil type.
  - Consider: maturity duration (short/medium/long duration), boll size,
    fiber length, and suitability for irrigated or rainfed conditions.
  - Do not use saved seeds from Bt hybrid cotton – hybrids do not breed
    true and performance will be poor in the second year.
  - For Bt cotton, maintain 20% refuge crop (non-Bt cotton or other
    susceptible crop) to comply with the Bt refuge requirement.

  SEED RATE:
  - Bt Hybrid cotton : 750 g – 1 kg per acre (approx. 450–500 g per packet)
  - Non-Bt Hybrid    : 1–1.5 kg per acre
  - Desi varieties   : 4–5 kg per acre

SECTION 6: SEED TREATMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Seed treatment is a low-cost but highly effective practice. Treating seeds
before sowing protects the young seedling from soil-borne diseases, insects,
and improves germination and early growth.

  STEP 1 – FUNGICIDE TREATMENT (for fungal diseases):
  - Carboxin 37.5% + Thiram 37.5% DS  @ 3 g per kg of seed
    OR
  - Thiram 75% WS                      @ 3 g per kg of seed
  - This protects against seed rot, damping-off, and early fungal infections.

  STEP 2 – BACTERIAL BLIGHT TREATMENT:
  - Streptomycin Sulphate 90% SP (100 ppm solution):
    Dissolve 1 g in 10 litres of water and soak seeds for 30 minutes.
    Then dry seeds in shade before sowing.
  - This protects against Bacterial Blight (Black Arm disease).

  STEP 3 – INSECTICIDE TREATMENT (for sucking pests):
  - Imidacloprid 70% WS @ 5–7 g per kg of seed.
  - Protects seedlings from aphids, jassids, whitefly, and thrips for
    the first 30–40 days after germination.
  - Note: Many Bt cotton seed packets come pre-treated with Imidacloprid.
    Check the packet label before applying again.

  STEP 4 – BIOFERTILIZER TREATMENT (optional but beneficial):
  - Azospirillum @ 25 g per kg of seed (nitrogen-fixing bacteria).
  - Phosphate Solubilizing Bacteria (PSB) @ 25 g per kg of seed.
  - Apply biofertilizers LAST, after chemical treatments.
  - Allow 8–10 hours between chemical seed treatment and biofertilizer
    application to prevent killing the beneficial bacteria.

  IMPORTANT: Always treat seeds in shade, not in direct sunlight. Dry
  treated seeds in shade before sowing. Never eat treated seeds.
  Keep away from children and animals. Wash hands after treatment.

SECTION 7: SOWING TIME
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Sowing at the right time is critical. Early sowing allows the crop to
establish before pest pressure peaks. Late sowing reduces yield.

  REGION-WISE RECOMMENDED SOWING TIME:
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Region                     | Irrigated Cotton  | Rainfed Cotton
  ---------------------------|-------------------|----------------------
  Gujarat                    | April 15 – May 31 | June 1 – June 30
  Maharashtra (Vidarbha)     | May 1 – June 15   | June 15 – July 15
  Telangana / Andhra Pradesh | May 1 – June 15   | June 15 – July 10
  Punjab / Haryana           | April 15 – May 15 | May 1 – May 31
  Rajasthan                  | May 1 – May 31    | June 15 – July 15
  Madhya Pradesh             | May 15 – June 15  | June 15 – July 15
  Karnataka                  | May 1 – June 30   | June 15 – July 15
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------

  KEY PRINCIPLES:
  - Early sowing (before monsoon) with irrigation gives higher yield
    because the crop benefits from a longer growing season.
  - Rainfed cotton should be sown within 2 weeks of the first good
    monsoon rain (when soil has 6–8 cm moisture).
  - Do NOT sow if soil temperature is below 18°C – germination will
    be poor and uneven.
  - Avoid sowing when heavy rain is forecast in the next 2–3 days –
    seeds may rot before germination.
  - Every 15-day delay in sowing after the optimal period reduces
    yield by approximately 5–10%.

SECTION 8: SOWING METHOD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  METHOD 1 – MANUAL DIBBLING (most common for small farmers):
  - Open furrows or pits at the required spacing using a stick or
    animal-drawn marker.
  - Place 2 seeds per hole, 2–3 cm deep.
  - Cover with soil and firm gently.
  - After germination, thin to 1 plant per hole at 10–15 days.

  METHOD 2 – SEED DRILL SOWING:
  - Use a bullock or tractor-mounted seed drill.
  - Sow at the required row spacing.
  - Good for uniform depth and spacing; saves time in large fields.

  METHOD 3 – RAISED BED SOWING (for irrigated cotton):
  - Form raised beds (60–90 cm wide) with furrows between them.
  - Sow 2 rows per bed.
  - Benefits: Excellent drainage, water saving (30–40%), better root
    aeration, and easier intercultural operations.

  SOWING DEPTH:
  - Ideal depth: 2–3 cm in moist soil.
  - Maximum depth: 5 cm in dry, sandy soil (to reach moisture).
  - Deeper than 5 cm: Poor and uneven germination.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 9: PLANT SPACING
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Correct spacing ensures every plant gets enough sunlight, air, water, and
nutrients. Overcrowding increases pest and disease problems.

  RECOMMENDED SPACING:
  ---------------------------------------------------------------
  Condition          | Row Spacing  | Plant Spacing | Plants/Acre
  -------------------|--------------|---------------|------------
  Irrigated, fertile | 120 cm       | 60–90 cm      | 400–550
  Rainfed, black soil| 90 cm        | 60 cm         | 750–800
  Rainfed, poor soil | 75–90 cm     | 45–60 cm      | 900–1200
  Desi varieties     | 60–75 cm     | 30–45 cm      | 1500–2000
  ---------------------------------------------------------------

  KEY POINTS:
  - Wider spacing for irrigated cotton as plants grow bigger.
  - Narrower spacing for rainfed, shorter-season, desi varieties.
  - Avoid overcrowding – it promotes diseases, pest buildup, and
    makes spray application difficult.
  - Gap filling: Check germination at 7–10 days. Re-sow any gaps
    immediately. Final plant stand should be at least 85–90% of target.
  - Thinning: Where 2 seeds were placed per hole, remove the weaker
    seedling at 10–15 days, leaving one healthy plant per hill.

SECTION 10: CROP GROWTH STAGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Understanding growth stages helps farmers apply the right inputs at the
right time and identify problems early.

  STAGE 1 – GERMINATION (0–10 days after sowing / DAS):
  - Seeds absorb moisture and sprout.
  - Seedling emerges with 2 cotyledon (seed) leaves.
  - Critical need: Adequate soil moisture and warmth.
  - Watch for: Poor germination, damping-off, seed rot.

  STAGE 2 – SEEDLING STAGE (10–30 DAS):
  - True leaves begin to form.
  - Root system develops rapidly.
  - Critical need: Gap filling, thinning, first weeding.
  - Watch for: Aphids, jassids, army worms, damping-off.

  STAGE 3 – VEGETATIVE STAGE (30–60 DAS):
  - Rapid leaf, stem, and branch growth.
  - Plant height increases significantly.
  - Critical need: First fertilizer top dressing, irrigation.
  - Watch for: Aphids, jassids, army worms, leaf feeding insects.

  STAGE 4 – SQUARING STAGE (45–70 DAS):
  - Flower buds (squares) appear on fruiting branches.
  - Very sensitive stage – any stress causes square shedding.
  - Critical need: Adequate nutrition, timely irrigation, pest control.
  - Watch for: Bollworms, thrips, spider mites.

  STAGE 5 – FLOWERING STAGE (60–90 DAS):
  - White flowers open; turn pink/red and fall after pollination.
  - Boll formation begins after pollination.
  - Critical need: Regular irrigation, balanced fertilization,
    pest control.
  - Watch for: Bollworms (most critical stage for bollworm damage),
    whitefly.

  STAGE 6 – BOLL DEVELOPMENT (80–130 DAS):
  - Bolls develop and fill with seeds and lint.
  - Major yield-determining stage.
  - Critical need: Irrigation, micronutrient spray, pest monitoring.
  - Watch for: Pink bollworm, target spot, boll rot.

  STAGE 7 – BOLL OPENING AND MATURITY (130–180 DAS):
  - Bolls split and open naturally, revealing white lint.
  - Plant begins to senesce (age naturally).
  - Critical need: Dry weather, no irrigation 3–4 weeks before harvest.
  - Watch for: Boll rot, staining from rain.

  STAGE 8 – HARVEST (160–200 DAS depending on variety):
  - Pick bolls when fully open and lint is fluffy and dry.
  - 3–4 pickings are typically done at 15–20 day intervals.
  - Avoid picking in wet/dewy conditions to maintain lint quality.

SECTION 11: COMMON PROBLEMS DURING CULTIVATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  PROBLEM 1 – POOR GERMINATION:
  - Causes: Old seeds, treated seeds stored too long, dry/wet soil,
    sowing too deep, soil crust after rain.
  - Solution: Use fresh certified seeds, check soil moisture, sow at
    2–3 cm depth, do gap filling within 10 days.

  PROBLEM 2 – NUTRIENT DEFICIENCY:
  - Nitrogen deficiency: Yellowing of older leaves starting from bottom.
    Apply urea top dressing.
  - Zinc deficiency: Small leaves, bronze/rust discoloration, shortened
    internodes. Apply zinc sulphate @ 10 kg per acre.
  - Boron deficiency: Hollow, malformed bolls, poor lint development.
    Apply borax @ 1 kg per acre or foliar spray.

  PROBLEM 3 – WEED COMPETITION:
  - Weeds in first 45 days are most harmful (critical weed control period).
  - Do 2–3 manual weedings at 15, 30, and 45 DAS.
  - Use pre-emergence herbicide: Pendimethalin 38.7% CS @ 700 ml per
    acre within 3 days of sowing (before germination).
  - Use inter-row cultivation with a bullock-drawn cultivator.

  PROBLEM 4 – WATERLOGGING:
  - Causes: Heavy rain, poor drainage, low-lying areas.
  - Symptoms: Yellowing, wilting, root rot, plant death.
  - Solution: Open drainage channels immediately. Form raised beds in
    waterlogging-prone fields before sowing next season.

  PROBLEM 5 – DROUGHT STRESS:
  - Symptoms: Leaf rolling, wilting in afternoon, premature boll/square
    shedding, poor boll development.
  - Solution: Irrigate immediately. Mulching between rows helps retain
    soil moisture. Foliar spray of 1% Potassium Nitrate can help.

  PROBLEM 6 – BOLL SHEDDING:
  - Causes: Extreme heat, drought, excess water, pest damage (bollworm),
    nutrient deficiency, or disease.
  - Solution: Identify cause first (pest vs environmental stress), then
    take appropriate action. Ensure regular irrigation and balanced
    nutrition.

SECTION 12: BEST FARMING PRACTICES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  1. SOIL TESTING EVERY SEASON:
     Get your soil tested before every season. Apply fertilizers and
     soil amendments based on the test result. This saves money and
     improves yield.

  2. USE CERTIFIED SEEDS ONLY:
     Never use uncertified or saved Bt hybrid seeds. Certified seeds
     guarantee germination percentage, varietal purity, and freedom
     from seed-borne diseases.

  3. FOLLOW THE RECOMMENDED FERTILIZER SCHEDULE:
     General schedule for irrigated Bt cotton per acre:
     - FYM / Compost     : 4–5 tonnes at land preparation
     - Nitrogen (N)      : 40–50 kg (split into 3 applications)
     - Phosphorus (P2O5) : 20–25 kg (full dose at sowing)
     - Potash (K2O)      : 20–25 kg (full dose at sowing)
     - First top dress: At 30 DAS with 1/3 nitrogen.
     - Second top dress: At 60 DAS (flowering stage) with 1/3 nitrogen.
     - Avoid excess nitrogen – it attracts pests and causes boll shedding.

  4. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM):
     - Monitor fields twice a week from germination to boll opening.
     - Use pheromone traps and sticky traps for early detection.
     - Spray only when Economic Threshold Level (ETL) is reached.
     - Use biological controls first; chemicals only when necessary.
     - Rotate chemical groups to prevent pest resistance.

  5. PROPER IRRIGATION MANAGEMENT:
     Critical irrigation stages (do not miss these):
     - At sowing (if dry)
     - 10–15 DAS (seedling establishment)
     - 45–50 DAS (before squaring)
     - 60–70 DAS (flowering – most critical)
     - 90–100 DAS (boll filling)
     Stop irrigation 3–4 weeks before final harvest for good boll opening.
     Drip irrigation saves 30–40% water and increases yield by 20–30%.

  6. INTERCROPPING:
     - Cotton + Cowpea (between rows): Cowpea fixes nitrogen and
       provides income while also attracting beneficial insects.
     - Cotton + Soybean: Common in Maharashtra (provides extra income).
     - Cotton + Maize (border crop): Acts as a trap crop for bollworms.
     These practices also make efficient use of land and reduce risk.

SECTION 13: TIPS FOR HIGHER YIELD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  TIP 1 – SOW EARLY:
  Early sown cotton (especially in April–May under irrigation) produces
  more bolls, escapes peak pest pressure, and gives higher lint yield.
  Target to complete sowing by May 31 in irrigated areas.

  TIP 2 – DO NOT SKIP SEED TREATMENT:
  Seed treatment with fungicide + insecticide + biofertilizer costs very
  little but protects the crop for the first 30–40 days. Healthy seedlings
  become strong plants that produce more bolls.

  TIP 3 – APPLY MICRONUTRIENTS:
  Zinc and Boron are critical for boll development and lint quality. Apply:
  - Zinc Sulphate @ 10 kg per acre in soil at land preparation.
  - Borax @ 1 kg per acre in soil.
  - Foliar spray of 0.5% Zinc Sulphate at squaring and flowering stage.
  - Foliar spray of 0.1% Boric Acid at flowering stage.

  TIP 4 – USE DRIP IRRIGATION:
  Drip irrigation with fertigation (fertilizer through drip) increases
  yield by 25–35% compared to flood irrigation, while saving water,
  reducing weed growth, and improving boll quality.

  TIP 5 – APPLY GROWTH REGULATORS (if needed):
  - Mepiquat chloride (Pix) @ 1–1.5 ml per litre of water applied at
    flowering stage reduces excessive vegetative growth (squaring/branching)
    and improves boll set in varieties showing excessive leaf growth.

  TIP 6 – PRACTICE CLEAN FIELD HYGIENE:
  - Remove all crop stubble and roots after harvest. This destroys
    overwintering pests (Pink Bollworm pupae in stubble) and disease spores.
  - Deep plough immediately after harvest and before the next season.

  TIP 7 – TIMELY AND PROPER PICKING:
  - Pick bolls only when fully and completely open – not before.
  - Pick in dry conditions (morning after dew dries).
  - Separate clean lint from contaminated or stained lint.
  - Store in a clean, dry place on raised platforms – not on the ground.
  - Use clean bags – avoid coloured polythene bags that contaminate lint.

  TIP 8 – MAINTAIN A FIELD DIARY:
  Record the date of sowing, fertilizer applications, pest observations,
  spray dates and products used, rainfall, and yield per picking.
  This record helps you make better decisions next season and is also
  required for government schemes and crop insurance claims.

  TIP 9 – USE GOVERNMENT SCHEMES:
  - Register crops under Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY) for
    crop insurance before the deadline.
  - Avail subsidies for drip irrigation under PM Krishi Sinchai Yojana.
  - Take soil health cards and follow their recommendations.
  - Get free extension advisory from Krishi Vigyan Kendras (KVKs) and
    state agriculture departments.
