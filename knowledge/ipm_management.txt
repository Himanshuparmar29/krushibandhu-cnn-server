SECTION 1: WHAT IS INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)?
================================================================================

Integrated Pest Management, or IPM, is a smart and practical way to control pests
and diseases on your farm. The word "integrated" means combining many different
methods together to fight pests — instead of depending on only one method like
chemical sprays.

IPM is based on a simple idea:
  "Use the right method, at the right time, in the right amount —
   to protect your crop without harming the environment."

IPM does NOT mean you never use pesticides. It means you use pesticides only when
truly necessary — and only after trying safer methods first.

IPM combines four main tools:
  1. Prevention — stopping pests before they arrive.
  2. Monitoring — watching your field regularly to catch problems early.
  3. Biological and cultural methods — using natural and farming-based solutions.
  4. Chemical control — using pesticides wisely, only when needed.

The goal of IPM is to keep pest populations BELOW the level where they can cause
serious damage — this is called the Economic Threshold Level (ETL). You do not
need to kill every single pest. You just need to keep their numbers low enough
that your crop is safe and your investment is protected.

SECTION 2: IMPORTANCE OF IPM IN COTTON FARMING
================================================================================

Cotton is one of the crops most affected by pests and diseases. In India alone,
cotton uses nearly 50% of all pesticides sold for agriculture — even though it
covers only about 5% of the total farming area. This is a serious problem.

WHY IPM IS IMPORTANT FOR COTTON FARMERS:

  ► SAVES MONEY:
    Following IPM reduces pesticide costs by 30–50%. Farmers spend less on
    chemicals and get better returns.

  ► PROTECTS HEALTH:
    Overuse of pesticides causes poisoning, skin problems, and long-term illness
    for farmers and their families. IPM reduces exposure to harmful chemicals.

  ► PREVENTS RESISTANCE:
    When the same pesticide is used again and again, pests become resistant
    (immune) to it. IPM rotates methods and chemicals so pests never become
    immune.

  ► PROTECTS BENEFICIAL INSECTS:
    Many insects help farmers — like bees (pollination), ladybird beetles, and
    lacewings (eat pests). Heavy pesticide use kills these helpful insects.
    IPM keeps the natural balance in the field.

  ► PROTECTS SOIL AND WATER:
    Pesticides seep into soil and water sources. IPM reduces chemical pollution
    and keeps soil healthy for future crops.

  ► IMPROVES COTTON QUALITY:
    Pesticide residues on cotton fiber reduce quality and market price. IPM
    produces cleaner cotton that meets export standards.

  ► SUSTAINABLE FARMING:
    IPM is good for the land, the farmer, the community, and the next generation.

SECTION 3: MONITORING AND EARLY DETECTION
================================================================================

Monitoring means regularly walking through your field and carefully observing your
plants. It is the MOST IMPORTANT step in IPM. You cannot manage pests you have
not seen.

HOW TO MONITOR YOUR COTTON FIELD:

  FREQUENCY:
  - Scout your field at least TWICE every week during the growing season.
  - Scout MORE frequently (every 2–3 days) during the flowering and boll
    formation stages, as these are the most vulnerable times.

  METHOD – The "W" WALK:
  - Walk through your field in a W-shaped path, covering different parts of the
    field equally.
  - Stop at 10–20 plants at random spots (not in a straight line).
  - Examine each selected plant carefully from top to bottom.

  WHAT TO CHECK AT EACH PLANT:
  - Upper surface of leaves: spots, patches, holes, discoloration.
  - Underside of leaves: insects, eggs, white flies, mites, aphids, mold.
  - Stem and branches: cracks, dark streaks, lesions, boring holes.
  - Flower buds (squares): look for holes, drooping, or early dropping.
  - Bolls: spots, soft areas, entry holes made by worms.
  - Soil near the base: caterpillars, pupae, or frass (insect droppings).

  TOOLS FOR MONITORING:
  - Small notebook and pen to record observations.
  - Magnifying lens (hand lens) to see tiny insects like mites and eggs.
  - Yellow sticky traps (10–15 per acre) to catch and count whiteflies and aphids.
  - Pheromone traps (species-specific lures) to monitor bollworm and army worm
    adult moths. Use 5 traps per acre. Check and record trapped moths weekly.
  - Light traps (1 per 5 acres) to attract and count night-flying moth pests.

  ECONOMIC THRESHOLD LEVEL (ETL) – WHEN TO ACT:
  Start treatment ONLY when pest numbers cross the ETL. Here are the ETLs for
  common cotton pests:

  Pest                    | ETL (Take Action When...)
  ----------------------- | -------------------------------------------
  Aphids                  | 10–15 aphids per leaf on 25% of plants
  Whitefly                | 6–10 adults per leaf on 25% of plants
  Thrips                  | 10–15 thrips per leaf
  Bollworm (American)     | 5–8% damaged squares (flower buds)
  Spotted Bollworm        | 5% damaged bolls
  Army Worm               | 5 caterpillars per plant
  Spider Mites            | 20% leaves infested

  If pest numbers are BELOW the ETL — do not spray. Monitor more closely.
  If pest numbers are AT or ABOVE the ETL — take action immediately.

SECTION 4: CULTURAL CONTROL METHODS
================================================================================

Cultural control means using good farming practices to prevent pests and diseases
from establishing in the first place. These are the cheapest and safest methods.

KEY CULTURAL PRACTICES FOR COTTON:

  ► CHOOSE THE RIGHT VARIETY:
    Use certified cotton seeds with resistance or tolerance to common pests in
    your region. Check with your local agriculture office or KVK for recommended
    varieties.

  ► PLANT AT THE RIGHT TIME:
    Sow cotton at the recommended time for your region. Late planting increases
    pest pressure. Early, timely planting allows cotton to grow strong before
    peak pest seasons.

  ► CROP ROTATION:
    Do not plant cotton in the same field every year. Rotate with wheat, chickpea,
    sorghum, or groundnut. Crop rotation breaks the life cycle of soil-dwelling
    pests and reduces disease buildup.

  ► PROPER SPACING:
    Maintain recommended row spacing (usually 90–100 cm between rows, 45–60 cm
    between plants). Good spacing improves air circulation, reduces moisture in
    the canopy, and lowers fungal disease risk.

  ► WEED MANAGEMENT:
    Remove weeds regularly. Weeds provide shelter and food for pests, and
    also compete with cotton for nutrients and water.
    Weed 2–3 times: at 20–25 days, 40–45 days, and 60–65 days after planting.

  ► NUTRIENT MANAGEMENT:
    Follow recommended fertilizer doses. Avoid excessive nitrogen fertilizer —
    it produces very soft, leafy growth that attracts aphids, whitefly, and
    other sucking pests.

  ► DESTROY CROP RESIDUES:
    After harvest, remove or deep plough all plant stems, roots, and leaves.
    This destroys the resting places of pests and fungi that survive for the
    next season.

  ► FIELD SANITATION:
    Remove dead plants, fallen bolls, and leaves from the field. Do not leave
    infected plant material lying on the ground.

  ► INTERCROPPING:
    Grow border crops or trap crops like:
    - Maize (corn): at field borders — attracts bollworms away from cotton.
    - Sunflower: attracts beneficial insects like lacewings.
    - Coriander, marigold, or fennel: attract predatory and parasitic insects.

SECTION 5: MECHANICAL CONTROL METHODS
================================================================================

Mechanical control means physically removing, trapping, or killing pests using
tools, traps, or manual labor. These methods are safe, chemical-free, and effective
especially when pest numbers are still low.

PRACTICAL MECHANICAL METHODS FOR COTTON:

  ► HAND PICKING:
    In small fields, collect and destroy caterpillars, egg masses, and infected
    bolls by hand. Drop them in a bucket of kerosene water to kill them.
    Best done early morning when pests are inactive.

  ► YELLOW STICKY TRAPS:
    Yellow color attracts whiteflies, aphids, and leaf miners.
    Use 10–15 traps per acre hung at plant height.
    Check traps every 5–7 days and replace when covered with insects.

  ► PHEROMONE TRAPS:
    These traps use chemical scents (pheromones) that attract male moths.
    When male moths are caught, they cannot mate — reducing egg laying.
    Use species-specific lures for: American Bollworm, Spotted Bollworm,
    Spodoptera (Army Worm), and Pink Bollworm.
    Rate: 5 traps per acre. Replace lures every 4–6 weeks.

  ► LIGHT TRAPS:
    Install 1 light trap per 5 acres. Moths are attracted to light at night and
    fall into the trap. Check and empty traps every morning.
    Helps monitor pest populations AND reduce adult moth numbers.

  ► BIRD PERCHES (T-SHAPED STICKS):
    Place T-shaped wooden perches throughout the field at a height of about
    1.5 meters above the plant canopy.
    Rate: 10 perches per acre.
    Birds like common mynahs, sparrows, and egrets land on perches and eat
    caterpillars, bollworms, and other pests. Completely free and natural!

  ► REMOVAL OF DAMAGED PLANT PARTS:
    Regularly remove and destroy heavily infested or diseased leaves, branches,
    and bolls. Do not leave them in the field — bury or burn them away from
    the field.

SECTION 6: BIOLOGICAL CONTROL METHODS
================================================================================

Biological control means using living organisms — insects, fungi, bacteria, or
viruses — to control pests. Nature already has a system of checks and balances.
Biological control works WITH nature instead of against it.

NATURAL ENEMIES TO PROTECT AND ENCOURAGE:

  PREDATORS (eat pests directly):
  - Ladybird beetles (ladybugs): eat aphids and whitefly eggs.
  - Chrysoperla carnea (Green Lacewing): larvae eat aphids, mites, whitefly,
    and bollworm eggs. Available for purchase and release.
  - Spiders: eat many types of insects.
  - Ground beetles: eat pupae and caterpillars on the soil.
  - Dragonflies and wasps: eat moths and caterpillars.

  PARASITOIDS (lay eggs inside or on pests, killing them):
  - Trichogramma spp.: tiny wasps that lay eggs inside bollworm and army worm
    eggs. Release 1,00,000 eggs per acre, every 10–15 days during flowering.
    Available from agriculture departments, KVKs, and bio-agent suppliers.
  - Bracon hebetor: parasitizes bollworm larvae.

  MICROBIAL PESTICIDES (microorganisms that kill specific pests):

  - Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt):
    A natural soil bacterium that produces proteins toxic to caterpillars
    (bollworms, army worms) but harmless to humans, animals, and beneficial
    insects.
    Dose: 500 grams per acre mixed in water.
    Apply in the evening for best results.

  - Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus (NPV):
    A virus that kills Helicoverpa (American Bollworm) and Spodoptera
    (Army Worm) caterpillars specifically.
    Dose: 250 Larval Equivalents (LE) per acre.

  - Beauveria bassiana:
    A fungus that infects and kills many pest insects including whitefly,
    aphids, thrips, and mites.
    Spray as a fine mist in the early morning or evening.

  - Trichoderma viride / harzianum:
    Soil and foliar fungus that protects against soil-borne fungal diseases
    and some foliar diseases like Target Spot.
    Use as seed treatment and soil drench.

  - Pseudomonas fluorescens:
    A beneficial bacterium that protects roots and leaves against bacterial
    and fungal diseases.

  HOW TO PROTECT NATURAL ENEMIES IN YOUR FIELD:
  - Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides that kill beneficial insects.
  - Grow flowering border plants to provide food (nectar) for beneficial insects.
  - Do not spray during peak beneficial insect activity (morning hours).
  - Use selective pesticides that target only the pest species.

SECTION 7: BOTANICAL PESTICIDES (PLANT-BASED SPRAYS)
================================================================================

Botanical pesticides are made from plants and plant extracts. They are safer for
humans and the environment compared to synthetic chemical pesticides. They break
down quickly in the environment and do not leave harmful residues.

IMPORTANT BOTANICAL OPTIONS FOR COTTON FARMERS:

  ► NEEM-BASED PRODUCTS:
    Neem (Azadirachta indica) is the most powerful and widely used botanical
    pesticide in India. It works against more than 200 pest species.

    How neem works:
    - Repels insects from the plant.
    - Disrupts the growth and reproduction of insects.
    - Reduces the appetite of insects (they stop eating the plant).

    Neem products available:
    - Azadirachtin 0.03% EC (commercial neem extract): 400–600 ml per acre.
    - Neem Seed Kernel Extract (NSKE 5%):
      Crush 5 kg neem seeds, soak in 10 liters of water overnight, filter,
      and dilute to 100 liters. Add a few drops of soap as a sticker.
    - Neem oil spray: 5 ml neem oil per 1 liter of water + a few drops of soap.

    Works well against: aphids, whitefly, thrips, mites, and young bollworms.

  ► GARLIC-CHILI SPRAY (HOME REMEDY):
    Crush 100 grams garlic + 50 grams green chili in 1 liter of water.
    Filter and dilute in 10 liters of water. Spray on plants.
    Repels many sucking insects.

  ► TOBACCO LEAF EXTRACT:
    Soak 500 grams dry tobacco leaves in 10 liters of water overnight.
    Filter and spray. Contains nicotine which repels and kills soft-bodied pests
    like aphids and mites.
    CAUTION: Toxic to humans — wear gloves when preparing and spraying.

  ► COW URINE SPRAY:
    Dilute fresh cow urine 1:10 with water and spray on leaves.
    Acts as a mild repellent and also has some anti-fungal properties.
    Traditional and cost-free remedy used by many farmers.

SECTION 8: SAFE CHEMICAL CONTROL
================================================================================

Chemical pesticides should be the LAST OPTION in IPM — used only when pest numbers
cross the Economic Threshold Level and other methods have not been sufficient.

PRINCIPLES OF SAFE CHEMICAL USE IN IPM:

  ► USE SELECTIVELY:
    Choose pesticides that target only the specific pest and do not harm
    beneficial insects. Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides when possible.

  ► USE CORRECT DOSE:
    Always follow the recommended dose on the label. Using more does not improve
    results — it wastes money and harms the environment.

  ► ROTATE CHEMICALS:
    Do not use the same pesticide every spray. Rotate between different chemical
    groups to prevent pests from developing resistance.

  ► SPRAY AT RIGHT TIME:
    - Spray in the early morning or late evening when beneficial insects
      (like bees) are less active.
    - Avoid spraying on windy days or before heavy rain.
    - Do not spray during flowering to protect pollinators.

  ► FOLLOW SAFETY PRECAUTIONS:
    - Wear gloves, mask, and long-sleeved clothing when handling pesticides.
    - Never spray near ponds, rivers, or water sources.
    - Store pesticides in a cool, dry, locked place — away from children.
    - Dispose of empty pesticide containers safely. Never reuse them.

  ► OBSERVE PRE-HARVEST INTERVAL (PHI):
    Stop all pesticide spraying at least 14–21 days before cotton harvest, as
    per the label instructions. This keeps residues out of the fiber.

  RECOMMENDED PESTICIDE GROUPS FOR COTTON IPM
  (Use only when ETL is crossed):

  Pest Target         | Pesticide Group          | Example (Generic Name)
  ------------------- | ------------------------ | -------------------------
  Sucking pests       | Neonicotinoids           | Imidacloprid, Thiamethoxam
  (aphids, whitefly)  | Organophosphates         | Dimethoate, Profenofos
  Bollworms           | Pyrethroids              | Lambda-cyhalothrin
  (caterpillars)      | Diamides                 | Chlorantraniliprole
                      | Spinosyns (semi-natural) | Spinosad
  Fungal diseases     | Triazoles                | Propiconazole, Hexaconazole
                      | Strobilurins             | Azoxystrobin
                      | Inorganic fungicides     | Copper Oxychloride, Sulphur
  Bacterial diseases  | Copper bactericides      | Copper Oxychloride
                      | Antibiotics (limited use)| Streptomycin + Tetracycline

SECTION 9: ADVANTAGES OF IPM
================================================================================

  ✔ LOWER FARMING COSTS:
    Fewer and smarter pesticide sprays mean less money spent on chemicals.
    Many IPM practices like crop rotation, sanitation, and biological control
    cost very little or nothing.

  ✔ BETTER FARMER HEALTH:
    Reduced use of toxic pesticides means less exposure to chemicals.
    Farmers and their families are safer.

  ✔ HIGHER QUALITY COTTON:
    Less pesticide residue on fiber means better quality and better market price.
    International buyers prefer cotton grown with IPM practices.

  ✔ HEALTHY SOIL AND ENVIRONMENT:
    IPM protects soil microorganisms, water quality, and biodiversity.
    Farms remain productive for future generations.

  ✔ SUSTAINABLE PEST CONTROL:
    Because IPM uses multiple methods, pests do not develop resistance as quickly.
    The same practices work year after year.

  ✔ SUPPORTS NATURAL ECOSYSTEM:
    Beneficial insects, birds, and natural predators thrive in IPM fields.
    This creates a natural, self-regulating pest management system over time.

  ✔ ELIGIBILITY FOR BETTER MARKETS:
    IPM-certified cotton can access premium markets, organic programs, and
    government support schemes.

SECTION 10: PRACTICAL FIELD TIPS FOR FARMERS
================================================================================

Here are the most important field-level tips for successful IPM in cotton farming:

  1. ALWAYS START WITH GOOD SEEDS:
     Buy certified, disease-resistant seeds from a trusted source.
     Treat seeds with Trichoderma + Pseudomonas before planting.

  2. SCOUT YOUR FIELD TWICE A WEEK — WITHOUT FAIL:
     Most crop damage can be prevented if caught early.
     Early detection = less damage + less money spent on treatment.

  3. KEEP A FARM DIARY:
     Write down what pests you see, when you see them, and what you did.
     This record helps you predict and prepare better next season.

  4. INSTALL PHEROMONE TRAPS FROM DAY ONE:
     Set up bollworm and army worm pheromone traps from the time plants
     emerge. This gives you advance warning of pest activity.

  5. PUT UP BIRD PERCHES:
     Install T-shaped perches (10 per acre) from the early vegetative stage.
     Attracting birds is completely free pest control.

  6. DO NOT PANIC AND SPRAY:
     Many farmers spray at the first sight of any pest. This is wrong.
     Check if pest numbers are above the ETL before spraying.
     Unnecessary spraying kills beneficial insects and wastes money.

  7. NEEM FIRST, CHEMICALS LAST:
     Always try neem spray or biological control first.
     Use chemical pesticides only when ETL is crossed and other methods fail.

  8. ROTATE YOUR PESTICIDES:
     If you use one pesticide this week, use a different type next time.
     This prevents pests from becoming resistant.

  9. COORDINATE WITH YOUR NEIGHBORS:
     Pests do not respect field boundaries. If you and your neighbors spray
     at the same time, you get much better results.
     Talk to your farming community about IPM practices.

  10. CONTACT YOUR LOCAL AGRICULTURE OFFICE OR KVK:
      If you are unsure about a pest or disease, take a sample or photo
      to your nearest Krishi Vigyan Kendra (KVK), agriculture extension
      officer, or use the KrushiBandhu AI assistant for guidance.
      Getting the right diagnosis is the first step to the right solution.
