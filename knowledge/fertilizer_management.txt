--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 1: IMPORTANCE OF PROPER FERTILIZATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton is a heavy feeder crop. It takes large amounts of nutrients from
the soil to produce leaves, stems, flowers, bolls, and lint. If the soil
does not have enough nutrients, the plant cannot grow well, produce enough
bolls, or give good quality lint.

Proper fertilization is important because:

- It directly controls how much yield a farmer gets per acre.
- Under-fertilization causes weak plants, poor boll set, and low yield.
- Over-fertilization (especially too much nitrogen) causes excessive
  leaf growth, more pests and diseases, boll shedding, and wasted money.
- Balanced nutrition makes plants strong and better able to resist pest
  and disease attacks.
- The right fertilizer at the right time saves input cost and improves
  profit per acre.
- Fertilizer applied at the wrong stage is wasted and can even harm the
  crop and the soil.

Key principle: "Right nutrient, right dose, right time, right place."
This is the foundation of good fertilizer management in cotton.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: NUTRIENT REQUIREMENTS OF COTTON
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton needs three types of nutrients:

  PRIMARY MACRONUTRIENTS (needed in large amounts):
  - Nitrogen (N)   : For leaf and stem growth, photosynthesis, protein
                     formation. Most important nutrient for cotton yield.
  - Phosphorus (P) : For root development, early plant establishment,
                     flowering, and seed formation.
  - Potassium (K)  : For boll development, fiber quality, disease
                     resistance, and water regulation in the plant.

  SECONDARY MACRONUTRIENTS (needed in moderate amounts):
  - Sulphur (S)    : Improves protein quality and helps in nitrogen use.
  - Calcium (Ca)   : Cell wall strength, root tip development.
  - Magnesium (Mg) : Central element of chlorophyll (green color of leaf).

  MICRONUTRIENTS (needed in small but critical amounts):
  - Zinc (Zn)      : Enzyme activity, growth hormone production.
  - Boron (B)      : Boll formation, pollen germination, fiber quality.
  - Iron (Fe)      : Chlorophyll formation (green color of leaves).
  - Manganese (Mn) : Photosynthesis support.
  - Copper (Cu)    : Enzyme function and reproductive growth.

  APPROXIMATE NUTRIENT UPTAKE PER QUINTAL OF SEED COTTON:
  - Nitrogen (N)   : 6–8 kg
  - Phosphorus (P) : 2–3 kg
  - Potassium (K)  : 6–8 kg
  This means a crop yielding 20 quintals per acre removes approximately
  120–160 kg N, 40–60 kg P, and 120–160 kg K from the soil.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: NITROGEN MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Nitrogen is the most important and most misused nutrient in cotton farming.
Getting nitrogen management right is the single biggest factor in cotton
yield improvement.

  ROLE OF NITROGEN:
  - Promotes rapid vegetative growth (leaves, stems, branches).
  - Drives photosynthesis and protein production.
  - Directly affects number of bolls and boll weight.

  RECOMMENDED DOSE:
  - Irrigated Bt cotton : 40–60 kg N per acre per season.
  - Rainfed cotton      : 20–30 kg N per acre per season.
  - Always based on soil test – reduce dose if soil is already nitrogen-rich.

  COMMON NITROGEN SOURCES:
  - Urea (46% N)                    – Most widely used, lowest cost.
  - Calcium Ammonium Nitrate / CAN  – Slower release, less burning risk.
  - DAP (18% N + 46% P)             – Provides both N and P at sowing.

  NITROGEN SPLIT APPLICATION – VERY IMPORTANT:
  Never apply all nitrogen at once. Split it into 3 equal parts:
  - 1st dose: At sowing (basal) – 1/3 of total N dose.
  - 2nd dose: At 30–35 DAS (seedling to vegetative stage) – 1/3 of dose.
  - 3rd dose: At 60–65 DAS (flowering / squaring stage) – 1/3 of dose.

  CAUTION – PROBLEMS WITH EXCESS NITROGEN:
  - Causes excessive vegetative (leaf) growth at the expense of bolls.
  - Makes plant tissue soft and tender – highly attractive to aphids,
    whiteflies, and other sucking pests.
  - Delays flowering and boll maturity.
  - Increases risk of fungal diseases.
  - Causes boll and square shedding.
  - Wastes money – excess nitrogen leaches into groundwater.

  SIGNS OF NITROGEN DEFICIENCY:
  - Yellowing of older (lower) leaves, starting from leaf tip and edges.
  - Slow growth, small leaves, pale green color throughout the plant.
  - Reduced number of squares and bolls.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: PHOSPHORUS MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Phosphorus is critical in the early stages of cotton growth. It drives
root development and helps the plant establish quickly after germination.

  ROLE OF PHOSPHORUS:
  - Promotes deep and strong root development.
  - Speeds up germination and seedling establishment.
  - Essential for flower formation and seed development.
  - Improves efficiency of nitrogen uptake.

  RECOMMENDED DOSE:
  - Irrigated cotton : 20–25 kg P2O5 per acre.
  - Rainfed cotton   : 12–16 kg P2O5 per acre.

  COMMON PHOSPHORUS SOURCES:
  - Single Super Phosphate / SSP (16% P2O5 + 11% S) – Best choice for
    cotton as it also provides sulphur. Apply @ 125–150 kg per acre.
  - DAP (46% P2O5 + 18% N)  – High analysis, less quantity needed.
  - Muriate of Potash + SSP – Combined with potash for basal application.

  APPLICATION METHOD:
  - Apply the FULL dose of phosphorus at the time of sowing (basal dose).
  - Mix well into the soil during the last harrowing, OR place in
    furrows before sowing.
  - Phosphorus does not move in the soil – it must be placed near
    the root zone at sowing time.

  SIGNS OF PHOSPHORUS DEFICIENCY:
  - Dark green to purplish-red discoloration on leaves (especially
    older leaves and stems).
  - Slow, stunted root growth.
  - Delayed flowering and maturity.
  - Low boll set.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 5: POTASSIUM MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Potassium is often called the "quality nutrient." It does not directly
create plant tissue but it controls many important functions that
determine lint quality and boll health.

  ROLE OF POTASSIUM:
  - Regulates water movement in plant cells (reduces drought stress).
  - Strengthens cell walls – makes plants physically resistant to pests
    and fungal diseases.
  - Improves lint length, strength, and fineness (fiber quality).
  - Critical for boll development and uniform boll opening.
  - Activates over 60 plant enzymes.

  RECOMMENDED DOSE:
  - Irrigated cotton : 20–25 kg K2O per acre.
  - Rainfed cotton   : 12–16 kg K2O per acre.

  COMMON POTASSIUM SOURCES:
  - Muriate of Potash / MOP (60% K2O)  – Most common, lowest cost.
  - Sulphate of Potash / SOP (50% K2O) – Preferred for quality-focused
    farming; also provides sulphur.
  - 00-00-50 (Potassium Sulphate)      – Used for fertigation (drip).

  APPLICATION METHOD:
  - Apply full basal dose at sowing, mixed with phosphorus.
  - Additional foliar spray of 1% Potassium Nitrate (KNO3) or
    0.5% Potassium Sulphate at boll development stage helps fill
    bolls and improve lint quality.

  SIGNS OF POTASSIUM DEFICIENCY:
  - Leaf margins (edges) turn yellow, then brown and dry – called
    "marginal leaf scorch."
  - Older (lower) leaves affected first.
  - Bolls are small and fail to open properly.
  - Poor lint quality – weak, short fibers.
  - Plants wilt quickly under drought or heat stress.

SECTION 6: MICRONUTRIENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Even though micronutrients are needed in very small quantities, their
absence can cause serious yield and quality losses in cotton.

  ZINC (Zn) – Most common deficiency in Indian cotton soils:
  - Signs: Young leaves are small with bronze or rust-colored patches.
    Short internodes (plant looks stunted and bushy). Delayed maturity.
  - Correction:
      Soil: Zinc Sulphate @ 10 kg per acre mixed in soil at sowing.
      Foliar: 0.5% Zinc Sulphate spray at 30–40 DAS and again at
              squaring stage (dissolve 5 g ZnSO4 in 1 litre of water).

  BORON (B) – Critical for boll development and fiber quality:
  - Signs: Hollow bolls, boll cracking before maturity, poor lint inside
    bolls. Terminal bud death in severe cases. Distorted young leaves.
  - Correction:
      Soil: Borax @ 1 kg per acre at land preparation (mix well).
      Foliar: 0.1–0.2% Boric Acid spray at flower bud (squaring) and
              early flowering stage (dissolve 1–2 g in 1 litre of water).

  IRON (Fe) – More common in alkaline soils:
  - Signs: Young leaves turn pale yellow to white while veins remain
    green (interveinal chlorosis on young leaves).
  - Correction:
      Foliar: 0.5% Ferrous Sulphate spray (5 g in 1 litre of water).
      Repeat every 10–15 days until color recovers.

  SULPHUR (S) – Often overlooked but important:
  - Signs: Young leaves turn pale yellow uniformly (unlike N deficiency
    which starts from older leaves).
  - Correction: Apply SSP (which contains 11% S) as phosphorus source.
    OR apply Gypsum @ 50 kg per acre at sowing time.

  GENERAL MICRONUTRIENT TIPS:
  - Conduct soil testing every 2–3 years for micronutrients.
  - Zinc and Boron are the two most critical micronutrients for cotton.
  - Never mix foliar micronutrient sprays with pesticide sprays without
    testing compatibility first.
  - Organic matter in soil improves micronutrient availability naturally.

SECTION 7: FERTILIZER APPLICATION METHODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  METHOD 1 – BASAL APPLICATION (At sowing):
  - Apply phosphorus, potassium, and 1/3 of nitrogen in the soil before
    or at the time of sowing.
  - Mix evenly into the top 10–15 cm of soil.
  - Most suitable for P and K as they don't move in the soil.

  METHOD 2 – TOP DRESSING (After crop emerges):
  - Apply remaining nitrogen in 2 splits (30 DAS and 60 DAS).
  - Broadcast urea evenly between rows, away from stem base.
  - Water the field immediately after urea top dressing OR apply before
    expected rain to avoid nitrogen loss through evaporation.

  METHOD 3 – FERTIGATION (Through drip irrigation – Best method):
  - Dissolve water-soluble fertilizers in the drip water tank.
  - Apply directly to the root zone in small, frequent doses.
  - Benefits: 30–40% fertilizer saving, 20–30% yield increase, uniform
    distribution, no soil disturbance, reduced leaching loss.
  - Recommended fertilizers for fertigation: Urea, MOP, SOP, MAP
    (Mono-Ammonium Phosphate), 12-61-0, 00-52-34, 13-40-13, etc.

  METHOD 4 – FOLIAR SPRAY (Directly on leaves):
  - Used for micronutrients and as an emergency correction of deficiencies.
  - Also used for quick booster at critical stages (flowering, boll fill).
  - Spray in the early morning or late evening – NOT in hot afternoon sun.
  - Use a clean sprayer and uniform coverage on both leaf surfaces.
  - Do not mix incompatible chemicals together.

  FOR RAINFED COTTON (per acre – reduced doses):
  -------------------------------------------------------------------------

  basis for fertilizer decisions.

SECTION 9: ORGANIC FERTILIZERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Organic fertilizers improve long-term soil health, add organic matter,
and provide nutrients slowly throughout the season. They are especially
important for maintaining soil structure and microorganism activity.

  FARM YARD MANURE (FYM):
  - Well-decomposed cow or bullock dung manure.
  - Apply 4–5 tonnes per acre during land preparation.
  - Provides N, P, K, micronutrients, and improves soil structure.
  - Must be well-decomposed (black, crumbly, no odour) before applying.
  - Fresh manure burns roots and introduces weed seeds.

  VERMICOMPOST:
  - Made by earthworms – higher nutrient content than FYM.
  - Apply 1–2 tonnes per acre at sowing time.
  - Also improves soil water-holding capacity and biological activity.

  NEEM CAKE:
  - Byproduct of neem oil extraction.
  - Apply 100–150 kg per acre in soil.
  - Provides organic nitrogen slowly over time.
  - Also repels soil insects and nematodes – dual benefit.

  GREEN MANURE:
  - Grow a fast-growing green manure crop (Dhaincha, Sunhemp, or
    Cowpea) before cotton and plough it under 45 days after sowing.
  - Adds nitrogen and organic matter to the soil naturally.
  - Improves soil structure and suppresses weeds.

  BIOFERTILIZERS:
  - Azospirillum (nitrogen fixer) @ 2.5 kg per acre in soil application.
  - PSB – Phosphate Solubilizing Bacteria @ 2.5 kg per acre – unlocks
    locked phosphorus in soil and makes it available to the plant.
  - Mycorrhiza @ 2 kg per acre – improves root nutrient absorption area.
  - These are low-cost, eco-friendly, and effective supplements to
    chemical fertilizers. They do not replace chemical fertilizers but
    improve their efficiency by 15–20%.

SECTION 10: FARMER TIPS FOR BETTER NUTRIENT USE
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  TIP 1 – ALWAYS DO A SOIL TEST:
  Get your soil tested at the beginning of every season. This tells you
  exactly which nutrients are deficient in your soil. Applying fertilizer
  without a soil test is like giving medicine without a diagnosis. The cost
  of a soil test (Rs. 50–100) is recovered many times over in savings on
  unnecessary fertilizers.

  TIP 2 – NEVER APPLY ALL UREA AT ONE TIME:
  Urea applied all at once is mostly wasted. The plant cannot absorb it
  all quickly. Split urea into 3 doses. This single practice can improve
  nitrogen use efficiency by 30–40% and visibly improve crop health.

  TIP 3 – APPLY UREA BEFORE RAIN OR IRRIGATION:
  Always apply urea 1–2 days before an expected irrigation or light rain.
  This dissolves the urea into the soil quickly and reduces nitrogen loss
  by evaporation (volatilization). Never apply urea when rain is heavy.

  TIP 4 – DO NOT NEGLECT ZINC AND BORON:
  Most Indian soils used for cotton are deficient in Zinc and Boron.
  These two micronutrients cost very little to apply but can increase
  yield by 10–20% and significantly improve lint quality and boll fill.
  Apply Zinc Sulphate in soil every 2–3 years and Boric Acid as a foliar
  spray at flowering stage every season.

  TIP 5 – USE SSP INSTEAD OF DAP WHERE POSSIBLE:
  SSP (Single Super Phosphate) is cheaper than DAP and also provides
  Sulphur (11%), which cotton needs for protein formation. In Sulphur-
  deficient soils, SSP is a better choice than DAP.

  TIP 6 – ORGANIC MATTER IS YOUR LONG-TERM INVESTMENT:
  Adding FYM, vermicompost, or green manure every season builds your
  soil's natural fertility year after year. Farmers who consistently add
  organic matter need 20–30% less chemical fertilizer over time and have
  healthier, more productive soil.

  TIP 7 – FOLIAR SPRAYS FOR QUICK CORRECTION:
  If you see a deficiency symptom (yellowing, spot, distortion), a
  foliar spray gives results in 5–7 days – much faster than soil
  application. Keep Zinc Sulphate, Boric Acid, and Ferrous Sulphate
  available for quick emergency sprays.

  TIP 8 – SWITCH TO DRIP FERTIGATION IF POSSIBLE:
  Drip irrigation with fertigation is the most efficient fertilizer
  delivery system for cotton. It uses 30–40% less fertilizer for the
  same or better yield compared to broadcast application. Government
  subsidy is available for drip systems under PM Krishi Sinchai Yojana.

  TIP 9 – KEEP A FERTILIZER DIARY:
  Note down every fertilizer application: date, product, quantity, and
  crop stage. This helps you track input costs, compare results season
  to season, and meet the records required for crop insurance (PMFBY).
