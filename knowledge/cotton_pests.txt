PEST 1: APHIDS (Aphis gossypii – Cotton Aphid / Melon Aphid)
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
------------
Aphids are one of the most common and damaging pests of cotton worldwide.
They are tiny, soft-bodied insects that live in large groups and feed by
sucking sap from the plant. A small number of aphids can quickly grow into
a very large population within days, making early detection extremely
important. In India, aphid attacks are commonly reported in cotton-growing
states like Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Punjab.

2. IDENTIFICATION
-----------------
- Size: Very small, about 1–2 mm in length (smaller than a sesame seed).
- Color: Usually yellow, light green, or dark green. Some may appear
  blackish or grey.
- Shape: Soft, pear-shaped body with two small tubes (called cornicles)
  sticking out from the back end.
- Found in clusters on the underside of leaves, on tender shoots, and
  on young buds.
- Winged and wingless forms both exist. Winged aphids spread the pest
  to new plants and fields.
- Sticky honeydew secretion on leaves is a key sign of aphid presence.

3. DAMAGE TO CROP
-----------------
- Aphids suck the plant's sap, which weakens the cotton plant.
- Leaves curl downward and inward (cupping effect) – this is a classic
  sign of aphid attack.
- Heavy feeding causes leaves to turn yellow, wilt, and dry up.
- Aphids produce a sticky liquid called honeydew. This promotes the
  growth of black sooty mould on leaves, which blocks sunlight and
  reduces photosynthesis.
- Aphids also transmit dangerous plant viruses like Cotton Leaf Curl
  Virus (CLCuV), which can destroy an entire crop.
- Boll formation is affected in severe cases, leading to yield loss of
  up to 30–40%.

4. LIFE CYCLE
-------------
- Aphids reproduce very fast. In warm weather, a new generation can
  appear in just 7–10 days.
- One female aphid can give birth to 50–100 young ones in her lifetime
  without mating (asexual reproduction).
- Both winged and wingless forms exist. Wingless aphids stay on the
  same plant; winged aphids fly to new plants, spreading the infestation.
- Under hot and dry conditions, populations can explode rapidly.
- The life cycle from birth to adult takes only 7–10 days.

5. CONDITIONS FAVORING PEST ATTACK
------------------------------------
- Hot and dry weather (temperature 25–30°C) with low humidity.
- Excessive use of nitrogen fertilizers (makes plants more attractive
  to aphids).
- Dense plant canopy with poor air circulation.
- Absence of natural enemies in the field.
- Early-season attacks (30–60 days after sowing) are most damaging.

6. PREVENTION
-------------
- Avoid excess nitrogen fertilizer application.
- Use certified, virus-free seeds.
- Maintain proper plant spacing for good air flow.
- Remove and destroy volunteer cotton plants and weeds (alternate hosts).
- Monitor fields regularly, especially the underside of leaves.
- Install yellow sticky traps (5 traps per acre) to detect winged
  aphids early.

7. CONTROL METHODS
------------------

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Deep ploughing in summer to destroy pest eggs and alternate hosts.
- Remove heavily infested leaves and shoots early.
- Avoid planting cotton near sugarcane or vegetables that harbor aphids.
- Intercrop cotton with coriander or marigold to attract beneficial
  insects.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Encourage natural enemies: ladybird beetles (ladybugs), lacewings,
  hoverfly larvae, and parasitic wasps (Lysiphlebus testaceipes) are
  natural predators of aphids.
- Release Chrysoperla carnea (green lacewing) @ 50,000 eggs per acre –
  very effective against aphids.
- Avoid broad-spectrum pesticides that kill natural enemies.

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- Spray only when aphid population crosses the Economic Threshold Level
  (ETL): 20–25 aphids per leaf or when more than 25% of plants show
  infestation.
- Recommended chemicals:
    • Imidacloprid 17.8% SL @ 100 ml per acre (systemic insecticide)
    • Thiamethoxam 25% WG @ 40 g per acre
    • Dimethoate 30% EC @ 300 ml per acre
    • Acetamiprid 20% SP @ 100 g per acre
- Use neem-based pesticides (Azadirachtin 1500 ppm) @ 600–800 ml per
  acre as a safer first option.
- Rotate chemicals to prevent resistance.

8. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
- Week 1–3: Monitor weekly using yellow sticky traps and scouting.
- Use neem oil spray (5 ml/litre) as a preventive measure.
- Release Chrysoperla carnea at first sign of aphids.
- Apply chemical spray only if ETL is crossed.
- Avoid calendar-based spraying – spray only when needed.
- Keep spray records and rotate chemical groups.

9. FARMER FIELD TIPS
----------------------
- Check the underside of young leaves every 3–4 days from the 30th day
  after sowing.
- If you see curled leaves or black sooty mould, aphids are likely
  already present.
- A strong jet of water can dislodge aphids from plants – useful for
  small infestations.
- Do not apply excess urea – it encourages soft, succulent growth that
  attracts aphids.
- Ladybird beetles are your friends – do not spray when you see them
  in the field.
- "Ek paudha, ek nazar" – inspect at least 10 plants per acre while
  walking through the field.

PEST 2: ARMY WORM (Spodoptera frugiperda / Spodoptera exigua – Beet Army Worm)
================================================================================

1. OVERVIEW
------------
Army worms are caterpillars (larvae) of moths belonging to the Spodoptera
group. They are called "army worms" because they move in large numbers
from field to field like an army, devouring crops in their path. They are
a serious threat to cotton, especially during the early vegetative stage.
Spodoptera frugiperda (Fall Army Worm) is an invasive pest that has
recently spread to India and can cause severe damage if not controlled
quickly. Spodoptera exigua (Beet Army Worm) is a long-established pest
in Indian cotton fields.

2. IDENTIFICATION
-----------------
- Eggs: Tiny, round, laid in clusters (100–200 eggs) on leaves. Covered
  with grey/brown hairy scales. Egg masses look like small cottony patches.
- Larvae (caterpillar): 
    • Young larvae: pale green or yellowish-green, small (2–3 mm).
    • Mature larvae: 35–40 mm long, greenish-brown to dark brown/black.
    • Distinctive inverted "Y" mark on the head (for Fall Army Worm).
    • Four dark spots arranged in a square on the last abdominal segment.
    • Lighter side stripes running along the body length.
- Pupa: Reddish-brown, found in soil (2–5 cm deep).
- Adult moth: Brown/grey forewings with white hindwings. 
  wingspan about 32–40 mm. Active at night.

3. DAMAGE TO CROP
-----------------
- Young larvae (1st–2nd stage): Scrape leaf surface, causing a
  "window-pane" effect – leaves look transparent or papery.
- Older larvae feed aggressively – completely skeletonizing leaves,
  leaving only the midrib and veins.
- Attack on growing point (apical meristem) causes "dead heart" –
  the central shoot dies, stunting plant growth.
- Damage to squares (flower buds) and bolls reduces yield directly.
- In severe attacks, entire fields can be defoliated within 2–3 days.
- Larvae also bore into stems and bolls in later stages.
- Yield losses can reach 20–70% in severe outbreaks.

4. LIFE CYCLE
-------------
- Complete life cycle: 30–40 days (egg to adult).
- EGG STAGE: 2–4 days. Eggs laid at night in groups on leaf surface.
- LARVAL STAGE: 14–21 days. The most damaging stage. Six larval
  instars (stages). Early instars feed in groups; later instars
  scatter and feed independently.
- PUPAL STAGE: 7–10 days. Pupation occurs in the soil.
- ADULT STAGE: 7–10 days. Female moth can lay 1,000–2,000 eggs in
  her lifetime.
- Multiple generations per year (4–6 generations in warm climates).
- Migrates long distances aided by wind.

5. CONDITIONS FAVORING PEST ATTACK
------------------------------------
- Warm temperatures (25–35°C) with moderate humidity.
- Cloudy weather and high rainfall can suppress natural predators,
  allowing army worm populations to rise.
- Dense crop canopy providing shelter and humidity.
- Monoculture farming (continuous cotton without crop rotation).
- Lack of natural enemies.
- Early season (0–45 days after sowing) is most vulnerable.

6. PREVENTION
-------------
- Use certified seeds and treated seeds (imidacloprid seed treatment).
- Deep summer ploughing destroys pupae in the soil.
- Crop rotation – avoid planting cotton after maize (a preferred host).
- Maintain field hygiene – remove crop debris after harvest.
- Install light traps (1 per acre) to attract and trap adult moths.
- Set up pheromone traps (Spodoptera lure, 5 traps per acre) for
  early detection and mass trapping of male moths.
- Avoid excess nitrogen application.

7. CONTROL METHODS
------------------

CULTURAL CONTROL:
- Deep ploughing exposes pupae to sun and predators.
- Flood irrigation can drown soil-dwelling pupae.
- Hand-pick and destroy egg masses and early instar larvae during
  morning scouting.
- Remove weeds (alternate hosts like Amaranthus, sorghum).
- Set up bird perches (10–12 per acre) – birds like mynas and starlings
  feed on exposed larvae.

BIOLOGICAL CONTROL:
- Spray Nuclear Polyhedrosis Virus (NPV of Spodoptera) @ 250 Larval
  Equivalent (LE) per acre – this is a virus that kills army worm
  larvae naturally and is very safe.
- Release Trichogramma pretiosum (egg parasitoid) @ 50,000–1,00,000
  per acre to destroy eggs before hatching.
- Bacillus thuringiensis (Bt) spray @ 500–700 g per acre – highly
  effective against young caterpillars.
- Encourage natural enemies: ground beetles, spiders, predatory bugs
  (Geocoris), and parasitic wasps.

CHEMICAL CONTROL:
- ETL (Economic Threshold Level): 5–8 larvae per row meter OR when
  10% plants show fresh damage.
- Recommended chemicals:
    • Chlorantraniliprole (Coragen) 18.5% SC @ 60 ml per acre
      (very effective, low toxicity to mammals)
    • Spinetoram 11.7% SC @ 200 ml per acre
    • Emamectin benzoate 5% SG @ 80–100 g per acre
    • Lambda-cyhalothrin 4.9% CS @ 200 ml per acre
    • Flubendiamide 39.35% SC @ 50 ml per acre
- Spray in the evening (larvae are active at night).
- Target spray towards growing point and inner canopy.
- Rotate insecticide groups to avoid resistance development.

8. INTEGRATED PEST MANAGEMENT (IPM)
-------------------------------------
Step 1 – Prevention: Deep plough + pheromone traps + light traps
         before and at sowing.
Step 2 – Monitoring: Scout twice a week. Look for egg masses,
         window-pane damage, and frass (caterpillar droppings).
Step 3 – Biological first: Release Trichogramma at egg stage.
         Spray NPV or Bt at early larval stage.
Step 4 – Chemical only if needed: Apply selective insecticides
         (Coragen/Emamectin) only when ETL is crossed.
Step 5 – Record-keeping: Maintain pest diary for better decisions
         next season.

9. FARMER FIELD TIPS
----------------------
- Scout fields early in the morning – army worms hide in soil or leaf
  folds during the day. Look for fresh green droppings (frass) near the
  plant base as a sign of larval presence.
- One pheromone trap per acre can help you know when adult moths are
  active – spray within 7–10 days of trap catches rising sharply.
- Birds are your best friend against army worms – set up bamboo
  perches across the field.
- Never spray the same chemical group twice in a row. Rotate between
  different chemical groups (diamides → spinosyns → pyrethroids).
- If more than 5 worms are found per plant in a cluster – it is already
  an emergency. Call your local agriculture extension officer immediately.
- Spray in the evening for best results – army worm larvae feed
  actively at night.
- "Pehle dekho, phir nirnay lo" – Always scout before spraying.
  Do not spray on a fixed schedule.
