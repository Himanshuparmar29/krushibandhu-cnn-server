SECTION 1: IMPORTANCE OF ORGANIC PEST CONTROL
================================================================================

Organic pest control means using natural materials — plants, animals, minerals, and
microorganisms — to protect your cotton crop from pests and diseases, without
using synthetic chemical pesticides.

For generations, Indian farmers managed their fields using local plants, animal
products, and traditional knowledge. These methods were not just customs —
they were practical, effective, and safe. Today, as the cost of chemical pesticides
rises and their side effects on health and soil become clearer, many farmers are
returning to these natural solutions.

WHY ORGANIC PEST CONTROL MATTERS:

  ► SAVES MONEY:
    Most organic remedies are made from materials available on or near the farm —
    neem leaves, garlic, buttermilk, cow dung, wood ash. The cost is a fraction
    of chemical pesticides.

  ► SAFE FOR FARMERS AND FAMILIES:
    Chemical pesticides cause poisoning, skin rashes, breathing problems, and
    long-term illness. Organic solutions are safe to handle and apply.

  ► NO RESIDUE ON COTTON FIBER:
    Chemical residues on cotton reduce its quality and market value. Organic
    methods leave no harmful residues — producing cleaner, premium cotton.

  ► PROTECTS BENEFICIAL INSECTS:
    Organic sprays target pests selectively and do not kill bees, ladybirds,
    lacewings, and other insects that help the farmer.

  ► BUILDS SOIL HEALTH:
    Many organic inputs (like cow dung spray and compost extracts) also improve
    soil microbial activity when they reach the ground.

  ► PREVENTS RESISTANCE:
    Pests do not develop resistance to natural organic preparations the way
    they do to synthetic chemicals.

  IMPORTANT NOTE:
  Organic remedies work best as PREVENTIVE measures and for MILD TO MODERATE pest
  pressure. In cases of heavy infestation (above Economic Threshold Level), organic
  methods should be combined with biological control and, if necessary, with
  approved selective pesticides. Organic control requires regular and timely
  application — do not wait until the attack is severe.

SECTION 2: NEEM-BASED SOLUTIONS
================================================================================

Neem (Azadirachta indica) is India's most powerful and versatile organic pesticide.
The whole neem tree — leaves, seeds, bark, and oil — has pest-repelling and
disease-fighting properties. It works against more than 200 species of insects,
fungi, bacteria, and nematodes.

HOW NEEM WORKS:
  Neem contains a compound called Azadirachtin. It works in multiple ways:
  - Repels insects from feeding on the plant.
  - Disrupts the growth and moulting (skin-shedding) of insects.
  - Reduces the appetite of caterpillars and other chewing insects.
  - Suppresses egg-laying by female insects.
  - Has mild anti-fungal properties against powdery mildew and other leaf fungi.

NEEM PREPARATIONS AND HOW TO MAKE THEM:

  ► NEEM SEED KERNEL EXTRACT (NSKE 5%) — Most Recommended:
    MATERIALS: 5 kg neem seeds (dried and de-husked)
    METHOD:
    1. Crush or grind the neem seeds coarsely.
    2. Soak them in 10 liters of water overnight (10–12 hours).
    3. Filter the liquid through a muslin cloth or fine net.
    4. Add 100 liters of water to the filtered liquid (dilute to 5% strength).
    5. Add 100 ml of soap solution (liquid soap or washing soap dissolved in
       water) as a sticking agent.
    6. Fill in a sprayer and spray immediately — do not store for more than
       8 hours as it loses potency.
    DOSE: Spray the full preparation per acre of cotton.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, whitefly, bollworms (young), thrips, mites,
    jassids, and caterpillars.

  ► NEEM OIL SPRAY:
    MATERIALS: 5 ml neem oil + 1 liter of water + 2–3 drops of liquid soap.
    METHOD: Mix well and spray directly on plants, especially the underside
    of leaves.
    DOSE: 500–600 ml of neem oil per acre, diluted as above.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Sucking pests (aphids, whitefly, mites, thrips).

  ► NEEM LEAF DECOCTION:
    MATERIALS: 5 kg fresh neem leaves.
    METHOD:
    1. Boil 5 kg fresh neem leaves in 10 liters of water for 20–30 minutes.
    2. Cool and filter the liquid.
    3. Dilute with 90 liters of water to make 100 liters total.
    4. Add soap solution as a sticker.
    5. Spray immediately.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, caterpillars, leaf-feeding insects, and mild
    fungal infections.

  SPRAY TIMING: Apply neem sprays in the early morning or late evening.
  Neem breaks down in strong sunlight, so avoid spraying in the midday heat.
  Repeat every 7–10 days during pest pressure periods.

SECTION 3: GARLIC-CHILI SPRAY
================================================================================

Garlic and chili are powerful natural repellents. Both contain strong-smelling
sulfur compounds and capsaicin (the heat compound in chilies) that repel and
irritate many pest insects without harming plants or beneficial insects.

HOW TO MAKE GARLIC-CHILI SPRAY:

  MATERIALS:
  - 100 grams of garlic (about 2 full bulbs)
  - 50 grams of green chili (5–8 fresh green chilies)
  - 1 liter of water
  - A few drops of liquid soap

  METHOD:
  1. Crush or grind garlic and green chilies together into a paste.
  2. Soak the paste in 1 liter of water overnight.
  3. Filter the liquid through a cloth.
  4. Dilute the filtered liquid in 10 liters of water.
  5. Add a few drops of liquid soap to help it stick on leaves.
  6. Spray on the upper and underside of leaves.

  DOSE: Prepare and spray per 10 liters of water per 10–15 plants.
        Scale up as needed for the full field.

  EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, thrips, whitefly, caterpillars, and mites.
  REPEAT: Every 5–7 days during pest pressure or after rain.

  TIP: You can add a small piece of ginger to the mix for added potency.
  Handle chilies carefully — avoid touching your eyes or face while preparing.

SECTION 4: BUTTERMILK SPRAY
================================================================================

Buttermilk (chaas/lassi) is a traditional Indian remedy used to control fungal
diseases and certain pest infestations on cotton. It contains lactic acid bacteria
that compete with and suppress fungal pathogens on plant surfaces.

HOW TO MAKE BUTTERMILK SPRAY:

  MATERIALS:
  - 1 liter of sour buttermilk (minimum 3–4 days old, fermented)
  - 10 liters of water

  METHOD:
  1. Mix 1 liter of sour buttermilk in 10 liters of water.
  2. Stir well and fill in a sprayer.
  3. Spray on both sides of leaves, especially when you see early signs of
     powdery mildew (white powder on leaves) or other fungal spots.

  DOSE: 10–15 liters of diluted spray per acre for early-stage disease.

  EFFECTIVE AGAINST:
  - Powdery mildew (white powder on leaves)
  - Mild fungal leaf spots
  - Acts as a mild foliar nutrient spray as well.

  REPEAT: Every 7–10 days during humid weather when fungal diseases are likely.

  ENHANCEMENT: Some farmers mix buttermilk with cow urine (1:1 ratio) for a
  more potent anti-fungal spray. Dilute 1 liter of this mixture in 10 liters
  of water before spraying.

SECTION 5: TOBACCO DECOCTION
================================================================================

Tobacco contains nicotine, a naturally occurring compound that is highly effective
against soft-bodied sucking insects like aphids, thrips, and mites. It acts as a
contact insecticide — insects die when they come in contact with the spray.

HOW TO MAKE TOBACCO DECOCTION:

  MATERIALS:
  - 500 grams of dry tobacco leaves or tobacco waste (stems and scraps)
  - 10 liters of water
  - 30 grams of soap or detergent

  METHOD:
  1. Soak 500 grams of dry tobacco in 10 liters of water overnight.
  2. The next morning, boil the mixture for 20–30 minutes.
  3. Cool completely and filter through a cloth.
  4. Add 30 grams of soap and stir well.
  5. Dilute to 30–40 liters total before spraying.

  DOSE: Spray 30–40 liters of diluted decoction per acre.

  EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, thrips, jassids (leafhoppers), mites, and other
  soft-bodied sucking pests.

  ⚠ IMPORTANT SAFETY WARNINGS FOR TOBACCO SPRAY:
  - Nicotine is TOXIC to humans and animals — handle with gloves, a mask,
    and long-sleeved clothing at all times.
  - Do NOT spray near ponds, water channels, or drinking water sources.
  - Keep away from children and pets.
  - Do NOT use on edible crops — for cotton use only.
  - Wash hands, face, and clothes thoroughly after preparation and spraying.

SECTION 6: BIOLOGICAL AGENTS
================================================================================

Biological agents are living organisms or their products used to control pests.
They are a key pillar of organic farming and work in harmony with nature.

KEY BIOLOGICAL AGENTS FOR COTTON:

  ► BACILLUS THURINGIENSIS (Bt):
    A natural soil bacterium whose spores and proteins are toxic to caterpillars
    (bollworms, army worms) when eaten. Completely harmless to humans, animals,
    birds, bees, and beneficial insects.
    HOW TO USE: Mix 500 grams of Bt powder per acre in water and spray in
    the evening (when caterpillars are active and feeding).
    BEST FOR: Young caterpillars in their first 2–3 stages.

  ► TRICHODERMA VIRIDE / HARZIANUM:
    A beneficial fungus that lives in soil and protects against soil-borne and
    foliar fungal diseases. It also promotes root growth.
    HOW TO USE: Apply 1 kg per acre as soil drench or foliar spray mixed in
    water. Also use as seed treatment (5–10 grams per kg of seed).

  ► PSEUDOMONAS FLUORESCENS:
    A beneficial bacterium that protects against bacterial blight and some
    fungal diseases. Also promotes plant growth.
    HOW TO USE: Seed treatment (10 grams per kg of seed) and foliar spray
    (500 grams per acre in water).

  ► CHRYSOPERLA CARNEA (GREEN LACEWING):
    A predatory insect whose larvae eat aphids, whitefly eggs, bollworm eggs,
    and mites. Release 50,000 eggs or larvae per acre.
    Available from agriculture departments, KVKs, and bio-agent suppliers.

  ► TRICHOGRAMMA SPP. (EGG PARASITOID):
    Tiny wasps that lay their eggs inside pest eggs (bollworms, army worms),
    killing them before they hatch into crop-damaging caterpillars.
    Release 1,00,000 (1 lakh) eggs per acre every 10–15 days during flowering.
    Available at KVKs and state government bio-control labs.

SECTION 7: HOMEMADE PEST CONTROL SOLUTIONS
================================================================================

These simple, low-cost solutions can be prepared at home from everyday materials:

  ► WOOD ASH SPRAY:
    Mix 1 kg of wood ash in 10 liters of water. Stir and filter.
    Spray on plants or dust dry ash on leaves.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, soft-bodied insects. Also supplies potassium
    to leaves.

  ► COW URINE SPRAY:
    Dilute fresh cow urine 1 part in 10 parts water.
    Spray on leaves early morning.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Mild fungal infections, repels some sucking insects.
    Also acts as a micro-nutrient foliar feed.

  ► SOAP WATER SPRAY:
    Dissolve 30–40 grams of plain soap (not detergent with chemicals) in
    10 liters of water. Spray directly on aphid and whitefly colonies.
    The soap blocks the breathing pores of soft-bodied insects.
    EFFECTIVE AGAINST: Aphids, whitefly, and spider mites.

  ► FERMENTED COW DUNG SPRAY (JEEVAMRUT):
    Mix 10 kg fresh cow dung + 10 liters cow urine + 2 kg jaggery + 2 kg
    pulse flour + 1 handful of field soil in 200 liters of water.
    Ferment for 48 hours, stirring daily. Filter and spray 200 liters per
    acre. Also works as a soil drench to boost soil microbial activity.

  ► CHILI-GARLIC-GINGER COMBINATION:
    Grind 50 g garlic + 50 g green chili + 50 g fresh ginger into paste.
    Soak in 2 liters of water overnight. Filter and dilute to 20 liters.
    Spray on plants. Repels a broad range of leaf-feeding insects.

SECTION 8: ADVANTAGES OF ORGANIC REMEDIES
================================================================================

  ✔ LOW COST: Most ingredients are available on the farm or in the local market
    at very low cost. Reduces input expenses significantly.

  ✔ SAFE FOR HUMANS: No risk of poisoning or long-term health effects for the
    farmer, family, farm workers, or consumers.

  ✔ NO WAITING PERIOD: Unlike chemical pesticides that require 14–21 days
    before harvest, organic sprays have no mandatory waiting period.

  ✔ RESIDUE-FREE COTTON: Produces clean, residue-free fiber that commands
    better prices in domestic and export markets.

  ✔ PROTECTS NATURAL ENEMIES: Beneficial insects — ladybirds, lacewings, bees,
    parasitic wasps — are not harmed, maintaining natural pest balance.

  ✔ BUILDS SOIL HEALTH: Organic inputs and bio-agents improve soil biology
    and structure over time.

  ✔ SUSTAINABLE: Can be used season after season without losing effectiveness.
    Pests do not develop resistance to organic materials the way they do to
    synthetic chemicals.

  ✔ SUPPORTS PREMIUM MARKETS: Cotton grown organically can be certified and
    sold at premium prices. Organic cotton commands 20–30% higher market price.

SECTION 9: SAFETY TIPS FOR ORGANIC REMEDY PREPARATION AND USE
================================================================================

Even natural and organic preparations must be handled carefully. Here are important
safety guidelines every farmer should follow:

  PREPARATION SAFETY:
  ► Always wear gloves when handling raw materials like chili, neem, and tobacco.
  ► Prepare sprays in a well-ventilated area — outdoors or in an open shed.
  ► Do not touch your eyes, face, or mouth while preparing sprays.
  ► Label all prepared liquids clearly. Never store them in food or drink
    containers.
  ► Keep all preparations out of reach of children and animals.
  ► Tobacco decoction is particularly toxic — handle with extra caution.

  SPRAYING SAFETY:
  ► Spray in the early morning or late evening — avoid spraying in hot sun,
    as this reduces effectiveness and increases evaporation.
  ► Always spray in the OPPOSITE direction of the wind — never into the wind.
  ► Wear a face mask or cloth over your nose and mouth while spraying.
  ► Wear long-sleeved clothes to protect skin.
  ► Do not eat, drink, or smoke while spraying.

  STORAGE AND DISPOSAL:
  ► Most organic sprays must be used within 8–24 hours of preparation.
    Do not store for long periods as they lose effectiveness and may ferment.
  ► Dispose of leftover spray away from water bodies, wells, and ponds.
  ► Wash sprayer equipment thoroughly after use.

  AFTER SPRAYING:
  ► Wash your hands, face, and arms with soap and water immediately.
  ► Wash sprayed clothing separately before wearing again.
  ► If any spray gets into eyes, wash immediately with plenty of clean water.
    Seek medical advice if irritation persists.
