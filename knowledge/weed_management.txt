SECTION 1: WHAT ARE WEEDS?
================================================================================

Weeds are unwanted plants that grow in your cotton field without being planted
by you. They grow on their own — from seeds left in the soil, blown in by wind,
carried by water, birds, or animals, or brought in with farm tools and machinery.

Weeds are a problem because they are STRONG survivors. They germinate fast, grow
quickly, and produce thousands of seeds that remain alive in the soil for many
years. A single wild plant like Parthenium (congress grass) can produce over
25,000 seeds in one season — all of which can sprout in future crops.

THE MOST IMPORTANT FACT ABOUT WEEDS AND COTTON:
  The first 45–60 days after cotton germination are the most critical period.
  Weeds that grow during this window cause the MAXIMUM damage to yield.
  Studies show that uncontrolled weeds in the first 6–8 weeks can reduce
  cotton yield by 40–80%. After this period, the cotton plant grows taller
  and shades the ground, naturally suppressing most weeds.

  This critical window is called the "Critical Period of Weed Competition."
  Keeping the field weed-free during this period is your single most important
  weed management task.

TYPES OF WEEDS BY LIFE CYCLE:

  Type              | Description                        | Examples
  ----------------- | ---------------------------------- | -------------------------
  Annual weeds      | Complete life in one season        | Parthenium, Wild oat
  Perennial weeds   | Survive many years, regrow from    | Nutgrass (Cyperus),
                    | roots or underground tubers        | Bermuda grass (Doob)
  Seasonal weeds    | Appear during specific season      | Most monsoon weeds

TYPES OF WEEDS BY APPEARANCE:

  Type              | Description                        | Example
  ----------------- | ---------------------------------- | -----------------------
  Grassy weeds      | Narrow leaves, grass-like stems    | Wild oat, Barnyard grass
  Broadleaf weeds   | Wide, flat leaves like regular     | Parthenium, Pigweed,
                    | plants                             | Black nightshade
  Sedges            | Triangular stems, grow in moist    | Purple nutsedge (Motha)
                    | soil                               | Yellow nutsedge

SECTION 2: COMMON WEEDS IN COTTON FIELDS
================================================================================

The following weeds are most commonly found in cotton-growing regions of India,
particularly in Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana, and Madhya Pradesh:

BROADLEAF WEEDS:

  ► PARTHENIUM (Congress Grass / Gajar Ghas):
    One of the most aggressive and damaging weeds in India. Grows very fast
    and produces thousands of seeds. Also causes skin allergies and breathing
    problems in humans and animals.
    Appearance: Whitish flowers, deeply lobed leaves, rough texture.

  ► PIGWEED (Chaulai / Amaranthus spp.):
    Fast-growing, tall broadleaf weed. Competes strongly for nitrogen and
    water. Common in almost all cotton-growing states.
    Appearance: Broad, oval green leaves, red to green fleshy stem.

  ► BLACK NIGHTSHADE (Makoi / Solanum nigrum):
    Grows rapidly in early season. Hosts several cotton pests and diseases
    even after the cotton crop is over.
    Appearance: Small white flowers, small round berries (green turning black).

  ► COMMON PURSLANE (Loni / Portulaca oleracea):
    Low-growing, fleshy-leaved weed. Spreads flat on the ground, competes
    with young cotton seedlings.
    Appearance: Small, thick rounded leaves, reddish stems.

  ► WILD JUTE / BROOM WEED (Corchorus spp.):
    Tall growing, competes strongly with cotton for light.

GRASSY WEEDS:

  ► BARNYARD GRASS (Jhangra / Echinochloa crus-galli):
    Very fast-growing grass weed that resembles young paddy. Major weed in
    monsoon cotton. Competes aggressively for water and nutrients.
    Appearance: Flat grass-like leaves, multi-branched seed head.

  ► WILD OAT (Avena fatua):
    Common in wheat-cotton rotation areas. Very competitive.
    Appearance: Tall grass with drooping seed heads.

  ► CROWFOOT GRASS (Eleusine indica):
    Low-growing, tough grass that spreads rapidly. Hard to remove by hand.
    Appearance: Star-shaped seed head at the top of the stem.

SEDGE WEEDS:

  ► PURPLE NUTSEDGE (Motha / Cyperus rotundus):
    One of the most difficult weeds to control in the world. Has underground
    tubers (nuts) that survive even after the above-ground plant is removed.
    Can regrow from tubers even if pulled out or hoed.
    Appearance: Triangular stem, purple-brown seed head, dark green narrow
    leaves.

  ► YELLOW NUTSEDGE (Cyperus esculentus):
    Similar to purple nutsedge but with yellow-brown seed heads.
    Also spreads through underground tubers.

SECTION 3: DAMAGE CAUSED BY WEEDS
================================================================================

Weeds damage cotton in many ways. Understanding how weeds harm your crop helps
you take weed management seriously.

  ► COMPETITION FOR NUTRIENTS:
    Weeds absorb the same nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium from the soil
    that you applied for your cotton plants. Studies show that dense weeds
    can absorb 30–50% of the fertilizer you apply — your money, going into
    weeds rather than cotton bolls.

  ► COMPETITION FOR WATER:
    Weeds absorb soil moisture, especially during dry spells. This stresses
    cotton plants and causes flower and boll drop. In rainfed cotton areas,
    weed water competition is especially damaging.

  ► COMPETITION FOR SUNLIGHT:
    Tall weeds shade young cotton plants, reducing photosynthesis (food
    making). Shaded cotton plants grow slowly, produce fewer branches, and
    ultimately fewer bolls.

  ► COMPETITION FOR SPACE:
    Dense weeds crowd cotton roots and reduce the space available for proper
    root development. Restricted roots mean the plant cannot absorb enough
    water and nutrients even if they are present in the soil.

  ► SHELTER FOR PESTS AND DISEASES:
    Many weeds act as alternative hosts for cotton pests and diseases:
    - Black nightshade hosts aphids, whitefly, and bollworm moths.
    - Parthenium hosts thrips and mites.
    - Grassy weeds shelter stem borers.
    These pests survive on weeds and then move to cotton when populations grow.

  ► INTERFERENCE WITH HARVESTING:
    Tall, tangled weeds make manual and mechanical cotton picking very
    difficult. They slow harvesting speed and increase labor costs.
    Weed seeds can also mix with cotton fiber during harvest, reducing
    fiber quality and market price.

  ► YIELD LOSS DATA:
    Uncontrolled weeds during the first 45–60 days can reduce cotton yield by:
    - Moderate weed pressure: 25–40% yield loss
    - Heavy weed pressure: 50–80% yield loss
    This translates to thousands of rupees of loss per acre per season.

SECTION 4: CULTURAL WEED CONTROL
================================================================================

Cultural weed control means using smart farming practices to prevent weeds from
establishing and growing in the first place. These are low-cost, preventive
strategies that every farmer should use as the first line of defense.

  ► USE WEED-FREE CERTIFIED SEEDS:
    Cheap or uncertified seeds often contain weed seeds mixed in with cotton
    seeds. Always buy certified cotton seeds from reliable sources with proper
    packaging and quality seals.

  ► TIMELY PLANTING:
    Plant cotton at the recommended time for your region. Timely-planted cotton
    germinates uniformly and covers the ground faster, naturally shading and
    suppressing weed growth. Late-planted cotton allows weeds to establish
    before the crop.

  ► OPTIMUM PLANT SPACING:
    Maintain recommended spacing (90–100 cm between rows, 45–60 cm between
    plants). Proper spacing allows the cotton canopy to close over the ground
    sooner, blocking sunlight from reaching weed seeds and slowing weed growth.

  ► CLEAN IRRIGATION WATER:
    Many weed seeds travel through irrigation water from canals and rivers.
    Use screens or filters on irrigation inlet points where possible, or allow
    water to settle in a small pit before entering the field.

  ► CLEAN FARM TOOLS AND MACHINERY:
    Weed seeds cling to tillers, cultivators, and harvesting equipment.
    Clean tools thoroughly after working in weed-infested areas before moving
    to a weed-free field.

  ► CLEAN FARMYARD MANURE (FYM):
    Raw (uncomposted) farmyard manure contains thousands of weed seeds from
    animal feed and dung. Always use well-composted manure where seeds have
    been killed by heat generated during composting (minimum 60–70 days old
    compost).

  ► SUMMER DEEP PLOUGHING:
    Deep ploughing the field in summer (April–May) before cotton planting
    exposes weed seeds and tubers to hot sunlight, drying and killing them.
    This is especially effective against nutsedge tubers and perennial weeds.
    Deep summer ploughing can reduce weed pressure in the following season
    by 30–50%.

  ► CROP ROTATION:
    Rotating cotton with crops like wheat, chickpea, or sorghum allows
    different weed control methods to be used, targeting weeds that thrive
    only in cotton-like conditions. Wheat, in particular, grows dense enough
    to smother most broadleaf weeds.

  ► COVER CROPPING AND MULCHING:
    After cotton harvest, sow a cover crop (cowpea, moong) immediately to
    suppress weeds and protect soil. During the cotton season, apply a
    5–8 cm layer of dry straw or sugarcane bagasse between plant rows as
    mulch. Mulch blocks sunlight from reaching the soil surface, preventing
    weed seed germination. A good mulch layer can reduce weed emergence by
    60–70%.

  ► INTERCROPPING:
    Growing a fast-establishing intercrop like moong bean or cowpea between
    cotton rows fills the empty ground quickly and suppresses weed growth
    naturally, while also providing the farmer with additional income.

SECTION 5: MECHANICAL WEED CONTROL
================================================================================

Mechanical weed control means physically removing or destroying weeds using
tools, implements, or manual labor. It is the most widely used weed control
method in Indian cotton farming.

  ► HAND WEEDING:
    Removing weeds by hand is the oldest and most thorough method.
    - First weeding: 15–20 days after sowing (DAS)
    - Second weeding: 35–45 DAS
    - Third weeding (if needed): 55–65 DAS
    Hand weeding is labor-intensive but very effective and safe.
    For small and medium landholdings, hand weeding is usually the most
    practical approach.
    IMPORTANT: Always remove weeds BEFORE they flower and set seed.
    A weed that flowers and sets seed multiplies the problem for future seasons.

  ► WHEEL HOE / HAND HOE (KHURPI):
    Using a hand hoe (khurpi) or wheel hoe to cut weeds just below the soil
    surface is faster than hand weeding and effective for inter-row weeding.
    Hoe between rows after each irrigation or rainfall, when soil is moist
    and soft — this makes hoeing easier and more effective.

  ► BULLOCK-DRAWN OR TRACTOR CULTIVATOR:
    For larger fields, inter-row cultivation using a bullock-drawn or
    tractor-mounted cultivator (dust mulcher or blade harrow) is efficient
    and fast.
    - First cultivation: 20–25 DAS
    - Second cultivation: 40–45 DAS
    Cultivator passes cut weed roots, loosen soil, and also improve water
    infiltration into the soil — giving you double benefit.
    NOTE: Cultivator passes only work between rows. Within-row weeds (close
    to the plant base) must be removed by hand.

  ► ROTAVATOR / POWER TILLER:
    On larger farms, a power tiller or rotavator between cotton rows gives
    faster and deeper weed control. Effective against both grassy and
    broadleaf weeds.

  ► STALE SEEDBED TECHNIQUE:
    Prepare the field 15–20 days before cotton planting. Give a light
    irrigation to encourage weed seeds in the top soil layer to germinate.
    Once the weeds emerge (as small seedlings), destroy them with a shallow
    cultivation or light flame weeding before planting cotton.
    This destroys the first flush of weeds before your crop is even in the
    ground — significantly reducing early-season weed pressure.

SECTION 6: CHEMICAL WEED CONTROL (HERBICIDES)
================================================================================

Herbicides are chemical products that kill or suppress weeds. They save labor
costs when fields are large or labor is unavailable. However, they must be used
carefully — the wrong herbicide, wrong dose, or wrong timing can damage your
cotton crop or leave residues in the soil.

ALWAYS follow these rules before using herbicides:
  - Read the label carefully before buying or using any herbicide.
  - Use the correct dose — more is not better and can damage the crop.
  - Use only herbicides registered for use in cotton in India.
  - Calibrate your sprayer to ensure even application.
  - Wear protective clothing, gloves, and a mask while spraying.

HERBICIDE CATEGORIES:

  A) PRE-EMERGENCE HERBICIDES
     Applied AFTER sowing but BEFORE cotton seeds germinate (within 2–3 days
     of planting). They form a chemical barrier in the top soil layer that
     kills weed seeds as they germinate.

  Herbicide Name       | Dose per Acre   | Target Weeds
  -------------------- | --------------- | -----------------------------------
  Pendimethalin 30% EC | 1.0–1.3 liters  | Grassy + broadleaf annual weeds
  Trifluralin 48% EC   | 400–500 ml      | Grassy + some broadleaf weeds
  Diuron 80% WP        | 400–500 grams   | Broadleaf + some grassy weeds

     HOW TO APPLY: Mix the required dose in 200 liters of water per acre.
     Spray uniformly on moist soil immediately after sowing. Do NOT disturb
     soil after spraying as this breaks the chemical barrier.

  B) POST-EMERGENCE HERBICIDES
     Applied AFTER weeds have already emerged (15–25 DAS) when weeds are
     small (2–4 leaf stage). More effective on young weeds.

  Herbicide Name           | Dose per Acre  | Target Weeds
  ------------------------ | -------------- | --------------------------------
  Quizalofop-ethyl 5% EC   | 400 ml         | Grassy weeds only
  Propaquizafop 10% EC     | 300–400 ml     | Grassy weeds only
  Pyrithiobac sodium 10% EC| 200–250 ml     | Broadleaf + sedge weeds
  Glyphosate 41% SL        | 800 ml–1 liter | ALL weeds (non-selective —
                           |                | USE WITH EXTREME CAUTION,
                           |                | directed spray only, avoid
                           |                | contact with cotton plant)

     ⚠ CRITICAL WARNINGS FOR POST-EMERGENCE HERBICIDE USE:
     - NEVER spray glyphosate or paraquat on or near cotton leaves or stems.
       These are non-selective and will kill cotton plants too.
     - Use a hood or shield on the sprayer nozzle for directed spraying
       between rows to protect the cotton plant.
     - Do not spray herbicides in strong wind.
     - Do not spray when cotton is under water or heat stress.
     - After spraying, do not irrigate for at least 24–48 hours.

  C) SPECIAL NOTE ON NUTSEDGE (MOTHA) CONTROL:
     Purple and yellow nutsedge are very difficult to control with regular
     herbicides because of their underground tubers. The best approach is:
     - Repeat deep summer ploughing to expose and dry out tubers.
     - Manual removal during initial weedings (pull out entire plant
       including tubers — do not just cut the top growth).
     - Halosulfuron methyl 75% WG (10 grams per acre) is effective against
       nutsedge. Consult your agriculture officer before use.

SECTION 7: INTEGRATED WEED MANAGEMENT (IWM)
================================================================================

Integrated Weed Management means using a combination of cultural, mechanical,
biological, and chemical methods together in a planned, season-long approach.
No single method alone gives complete weed control — but combining methods
wisely gives the best results at the lowest cost.

THE IWM APPROACH FOR COTTON:

  BEFORE PLANTING:
  ► Deep summer ploughing (April–May) to kill weed seeds and tubers.
  ► Prepare a stale seedbed — irrigate, allow weeds to germinate, then
    destroy them with cultivation before sowing cotton.
  ► Apply well-composted FYM only (not raw manure with weed seeds).

  AT PLANTING:
  ► Use certified, weed-seed-free cotton seeds.
  ► Apply a pre-emergence herbicide (Pendimethalin) within 2–3 days of
    sowing on moist soil.
  ► Apply mulch between rows after planting to block weed germination.

  EARLY SEASON (15–45 DAS — THE CRITICAL PERIOD):
  ► First hand weeding or hoeing at 15–20 DAS.
  ► Second hand weeding or tractor cultivation at 35–45 DAS.
  ► If grassy weeds persist after hand weeding, apply a post-emergence
    graminicide (grass killer) like Quizalofop at 15–20 DAS.

  MID SEASON (45–75 DAS):
  ► Third hand weeding or cultivation at 55–65 DAS if weed pressure
    continues.
  ► Cotton canopy will begin to close at this stage — naturally suppressing
    further weed growth.

  LATE SEASON (75+ DAS):
  ► Minimal weeding needed once the cotton canopy closes.
  ► Remove any tall, flowering weeds before they shed seeds into the soil.

  AFTER HARVEST:
  ► Destroy or compost all weed material from the field.
  ► Do NOT allow weeds to flower and set seed before the field is cleared.
  ► Deep plough the field immediately after harvest.

IWM BENEFITS:
  - Reduces herbicide use by 40–60% compared to relying on chemicals alone.
  - Breaks weed life cycles across seasons.
  - Lowers weed seed bank in the soil over 2–3 years of consistent practice.
  - Reduces input cost while maintaining or improving cotton yield.
  - Prevents herbicide resistance in weed populations.

SECTION 8: BEST PRACTICES FOR FARMERS
================================================================================

Here are the most practical, field-tested tips for successful weed management
in cotton farming:

  1. WEED EARLY AND WEED OFTEN IN THE FIRST 45 DAYS:
     This is the single most important rule. Every extra day you delay
     weeding in the first 45 days costs you yield. Set aside labor and
     budget for at least two weedings in this period.

  2. NEVER LET WEEDS FLOWER IN YOUR FIELD:
     A weed that flowers adds thousands of seeds to your soil for future
     seasons. Remove or cut weeds before they can flower and set seed,
     even if you cannot remove all weeds completely.

  3. DO SUMMER DEEP PLOUGHING EVERY YEAR:
     This one field preparation step dramatically reduces weed pressure
     in the next cotton season. It is especially effective against nutsedge.

  4. USE MULCH BETWEEN ROWS:
     Applying dry straw or other organic mulch between cotton rows after
     planting is one of the best investments you can make. It controls weeds,
     saves water, and adds organic matter to the soil — three benefits at once.

  5. CALIBRATE YOUR SPRAYER BEFORE HERBICIDE APPLICATION:
     Check that your sprayer delivers the correct volume per area. An
     uncalibrated sprayer can apply too much (crop damage) or too little
     (poor weed control).

  6. KEEP FIELD BORDERS AND BUNDS CLEAN:
     Weeds on field borders and bunds produce seeds that blow into your
     field. Cut or remove weeds on field boundaries before they flower,
     every season.

  7. ROTATE HERBICIDE GROUPS:
     If you use herbicides, rotate between different chemical groups each
     season. Using the same herbicide repeatedly selects for resistant weeds.

  8. DO NOT BURN WEEDS IN THE FIELD:
     Burning weeds is fast but harmful. It destroys soil organic matter,
     kills beneficial soil organisms, and releases air pollutants. Compost
     or bury weed material instead.

  9. USE INTERCROPPING AS A WEED SUPPRESSION STRATEGY:
     A quick-growing intercrop like moong bean sown between cotton rows
     covers the ground fast, suppressing weeds naturally while also
     generating additional income.

  10. MAINTAIN RECORDS OF WEED PROBLEMS:
      Note which weeds are most common in your field, which areas are worst,
      and which control methods worked best. This record helps you plan more
      effectively for the next season.
