SECTION 1: IMPORTANCE OF PROPER IRRIGATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Water is the most important input in cotton farming after seeds and
fertilizers. Cotton cannot grow, flower, or produce bolls without the
right amount of water at the right time. Too little water causes the
plant to wilt, shed flowers and bolls, and produce very low yield. Too
much water is equally harmful — it waterloggs the roots, causes diseases,
and wastes a precious resource.

Proper irrigation management matters because:

- Cotton yield is directly linked to water availability at key growth
  stages. Missing irrigation at the wrong time can reduce yield by 30–50%.
- Water is becoming scarce in most cotton-growing areas of India. Using
  water efficiently saves money and protects groundwater.
- Over-irrigation leaches away valuable nutrients (especially nitrogen)
  from the root zone, wasting fertilizers and money.
- Waterlogged soils promote root rot, fungal diseases, and invite pest
  attack on stressed plants.
- Proper irrigation reduces stress on the crop and produces more uniform
  boll development, better lint quality, and higher market price.
- With the right irrigation system (especially drip), farmers can save
  30–50% water and still get 20–30% higher yield compared to flood
  irrigation.

Key principle: "Irrigate based on crop need, not on habit or calendar."

SECTION 2: WATER REQUIREMENTS OF COTTON
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton is a moderately drought-tolerant crop but still needs a significant
amount of water for good yield. Water requirement changes at every stage
of the crop.

  TOTAL WATER REQUIREMENT:
  - Rainfed cotton      : 500–700 mm of rainfall per season is enough
                          if well distributed.
  - Irrigated cotton    : 700–1000 mm total water (rainfall + irrigation)
                          across the full growing season of 150–180 days.

  WATER NEED BY CROP STAGE:
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  Growth Stage         | Duration      | Water Need   | Sensitivity
  ---------------------|---------------|--------------|------------------
  Germination          | 0–10 DAS      | Low–Moderate | High (soil must
                       |               |              | be moist, not wet)
  Seedling             | 10–30 DAS     | Low          | Moderate
  Vegetative           | 30–60 DAS     | Moderate     | Moderate
  Squaring             | 45–70 DAS     | Moderate     | High
  Flowering            | 60–90 DAS     | HIGH         | Very High
  Boll Development     | 80–130 DAS    | HIGH         | Very High
  Boll Opening/Mature  | 130–180 DAS   | Low          | Moderate
                       |               | (reduce now) |
  -----------------------------------------------------------------------
  DAS = Days After Sowing

  KEY WATER FACTS:
  - Flowering and boll development stages are the most water-sensitive.
    Water stress at these stages causes the maximum yield loss.
  - Cotton can tolerate mild water stress during vegetative stage better
    than any other stage.
  - Excess water during boll opening reduces lint quality and causes
    staining, mould, and boll rot.

SECTION 3: CRITICAL IRRIGATION STAGES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Not all irrigations are equally important. Missing some can be managed,
but missing these critical stages causes permanent yield loss.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 1 – PRE-SOWING IRRIGATION:
  - Purpose: To bring soil moisture to field capacity before sowing.
  - When: 7–10 days before sowing in dry areas (April–May).
  - Result: Ensures uniform germination and strong seedling emergence.
  - Skip if: Soil already has adequate moisture from recent rain.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 2 – SEEDLING ESTABLISHMENT (10–15 DAS):
  - Purpose: To support young root development.
  - When: 10–15 days after sowing if no rain.
  - Result: Reduces seedling mortality, ensures uniform plant stand.
  - Caution: Light irrigation only – do not flood young seedlings.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 3 – VEGETATIVE STAGE (30–40 DAS):
  - Purpose: To support rapid leaf and branch development.
  - When: 30–40 days after sowing.
  - Result: Builds a strong plant framework that will support more bolls.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 4 – SQUARING STAGE (45–55 DAS):
  - Purpose: To support formation of flower buds (squares).
  - When: When first squares appear (around 45–55 DAS).
  - Result: Prevents premature square shedding. More squares = more bolls.
  - This is a HIGH PRIORITY irrigation. Do not miss.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 5 – FLOWERING STAGE (60–75 DAS):
  - Purpose: To support open flowers and early boll set.
  - When: At first flower opening and continuing through peak flowering.
  - Result: Maximum boll retention. This is the MOST CRITICAL irrigation.
  - Water stress at this stage causes flower drop and empty bolls.
  - Yield loss from missing this: up to 40–50%.

  CRITICAL IRRIGATION 6 – BOLL DEVELOPMENT STAGE (90–110 DAS):
  - Purpose: To fill bolls with lint and seeds.
  - When: When bolls are actively swelling (green boll stage).
  - Result: Heavier bolls, better lint length and strength.
  - This is the second most critical irrigation stage.

  STOP IRRIGATION (3–4 weeks before final picking):
  - Purpose: To allow bolls to dry, split, and open uniformly.
  - Result: Cleaner lint, better quality, easier picking.
  - Over-irrigating at this stage causes boll rot, staining, and delay.

  IRRIGATION INTERVAL GUIDE (General):
  - Sandy / light soils  : Every 7–10 days during peak need.
  - Black cotton soil    : Every 12–15 days (holds more water).
  - Use soil finger test : Push finger 5 cm into soil. If dry at that
    depth, irrigate. If moist, wait.

SECTION 4: IRRIGATION METHODS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  METHOD 1 – DRIP IRRIGATION (BEST METHOD FOR COTTON):
  -------------------------------------------------------
  Drip irrigation delivers water slowly and directly to the root zone
  through a network of pipes and drippers placed on or below the soil
  surface.

  HOW IT WORKS:
  - Drippers placed at 60 cm intervals along the cotton rows.
  - Water flows at a slow rate (2–4 litres per hour per dripper).
  - Keeps the root zone consistently moist without waterlogging.

  ADVANTAGES:
  - Saves 40–50% water compared to flood irrigation.
  - Increases yield by 20–35% due to consistent moisture and fertigation.
  - Reduces weed growth (only root zone is wet, inter-row area stays dry).
  - Enables fertigation – applying fertilizers through drip water, saving
    30–40% on fertilizer costs.
  - Reduces fungal disease risk (leaves and soil surface stay dry).
  - Suitable for water-scarce and drought-prone regions.

  DISADVANTAGES:
  - Higher initial installation cost (Rs. 30,000–50,000 per acre).
  - Government subsidy available under PM Krishi Sinchai Yojana (PMKSY)
    covering 55–90% of the cost for small and marginal farmers.
  - Requires regular maintenance to prevent clogging of drippers.

  RECOMMENDATION: Drip irrigation is strongly recommended for all
  irrigated cotton areas. The investment pays back in 2–3 seasons.

  -------------------------------------------------------
  METHOD 2 – FURROW IRRIGATION (FLOOD / SURFACE IRRIGATION):
  -------------------------------------------------------
  Water flows along furrows (channels) made between crop rows.

  HOW IT WORKS:
  - Furrows are made between cotton rows at sowing.
  - Water is released into furrows from the irrigation channel.
  - Water soaks into the soil from the sides of the furrow.

  ADVANTAGES:
  - Low cost – no equipment needed beyond the furrows.
  - Suitable for clay and medium soils that hold water well.
  - Most widely used method in traditional cotton farming.

  DISADVANTAGES:
  - Uses 2–3 times more water than drip.
  - Risk of waterlogging if not managed carefully.
  - Uneven distribution in sloping or irregular fields.
  - Water runs off the end of furrows if not controlled (wastage).

  BEST PRACTICE FOR FURROW IRRIGATION:
  - Make furrows on contour (follow the slope) to reduce runoff.
  - Irrigate only alternate furrows in one irrigation and remaining
    furrows in the next (Alternate Furrow Irrigation – saves 25–30%
    water with minimal yield loss).
  - Stop water before it reaches the end of the furrow to prevent
    runoff and ensure even distribution.

  -------------------------------------------------------
  METHOD 3 – SPRINKLER IRRIGATION:
  -------------------------------------------------------
  Water is pumped through pipes and sprayed over the crop like rain
  through rotating nozzles.

  ADVANTAGES:
  - More uniform distribution than flood irrigation.
  - Saves 25–35% water compared to flood irrigation.
  - Useful for light sandy soils where furrow irrigation is difficult.
  - Can also be used for cooling the crop during extreme heat.

  DISADVANTAGES:
  - Not ideal for cotton at flowering stage – wetting flowers can
    cause them to drop and reduce boll set.
  - Increases humidity around the crop, which can promote fungal
    diseases (powdery mildew, target spot).
  - Less water-efficient than drip for cotton.

  RECOMMENDATION: Sprinkler is suitable for germination and vegetative
  stages. Avoid sprinkler during flowering and boll stage.

SECTION 5: AVOIDING OVER-IRRIGATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Over-irrigation is as harmful as drought in cotton. Many farmers over-
irrigate thinking more water equals more yield — this is not true.

  SIGNS OF OVER-IRRIGATION:
  - Yellowing of leaves (especially lower, older leaves).
  - Wilting even when soil is wet (roots cannot absorb oxygen in
    waterlogged soil).
  - Stunted growth and pale green color.
  - Fungal diseases appearing rapidly (root rot, boll rot, target spot).
  - Fruit (square and boll) shedding increases.
  - Foul smell from soil (anaerobic / oxygen-deprived conditions).

  CAUSES OF OVER-IRRIGATION:
  - Irrigating on a fixed schedule without checking soil moisture.
  - Using flood irrigation without monitoring furrow end flow.
  - Not accounting for rainfall in the irrigation schedule.
  - Irrigating before the previous irrigation has been fully used.

  HOW TO AVOID OVER-IRRIGATION:
  1. Use the FINGER TEST: Push finger 5 cm into soil. Irrigate only
     when soil is dry at that depth between rows.
  2. Install a simple rain gauge in the field. Subtract recent rainfall
     from the irrigation schedule.
  3. For furrow irrigation: Stop water flow when 80% of the furrow
     length is covered (remaining 20% will wet by capillary action).
  4. Use drip or furrow — never flood the entire field.
  5. Ensure field drainage channels are open before every irrigation.

SECTION 6: DROUGHT MANAGEMENT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Cotton is moderately tolerant of dry conditions, but prolonged drought
— especially during flowering and boll stages — can severely damage the
crop. When water is limited, a smart approach is needed.

  SIGNS OF DROUGHT STRESS IN COTTON:
  - Leaves curl and roll inward during the day (uncurl at night).
  - Wilting of shoot tips in the afternoon.
  - Square and flower shedding increases.
  - Leaf color changes to dull grey-green.
  - Growth stops or slows significantly.

  DROUGHT MANAGEMENT STRATEGIES:

  1. PRIORITIZE CRITICAL STAGES:
     If water is limited, always irrigate first at flowering and boll
     development stage. These stages give the highest return per litre
     of water applied.

  2. SOIL MOISTURE CONSERVATION:
     - Apply dry soil mulch (loose dry soil) between rows after irrigation
       to seal surface cracks and reduce evaporation.
     - Intercultural operations (hoeing) break soil crust and reduce
       water loss by 20–25%.
     - Apply organic mulch (dry grass, crop residue) @ 2 tonnes per acre
       between rows. Reduces soil temperature and water evaporation.

  3. ANTI-TRANSPIRANT SPRAY:
     - Spray Kaolin 5% OR 1% Potassium Chloride on leaves during drought
       to reduce water loss from leaves (transpiration).
     - This gives temporary relief during severe moisture stress.

  4. FOLIAR NUTRITION DURING DROUGHT:
     - Spray 1% Potassium Nitrate (KNO3) on leaves during drought stress.
     - Potassium helps the plant regulate water use more efficiently.

  5. SKIP IRRIGATION AT LOW-IMPACT STAGES:
     - Vegetative stage (30–50 DAS) can tolerate mild drought better than
       other stages. If water is very limited, this stage can be skipped
       in rainfed cotton without severe yield loss.

SECTION 7: WATER CONSERVATION TECHNIQUES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Water conservation in cotton farming protects both the farmer's cost and
the long-term availability of groundwater in the region.

  TECHNIQUE 1 – MULCHING:
  - Spread dry crop residue, grass, or plastic mulch (25–30 micron)
    between cotton rows.
  - Reduces soil surface evaporation by 40–50%.
  - Maintains soil temperature and improves root growth.
  - Plastic mulch also controls weeds effectively.

  TECHNIQUE 2 – RAISED BED PLANTING:
  - Sow cotton on raised beds (60–90 cm wide) with furrows between beds.
  - Water is applied only in furrows — not flooded over the beds.
  - Saves 25–30% water, improves drainage, reduces waterlogging risk.

  TECHNIQUE 3 – CONTOUR FARMING:
  - On sloping land, make ridges and furrows along the contour lines
    (across the slope, not down the slope).
  - Prevents water runoff and allows more water to soak into the soil.

  TECHNIQUE 4 – FARM PONDS:
  - Collect rainwater in on-farm ponds during monsoon.
  - Use stored water for critical irrigations during dry spells.
  - Government subsidy available for farm pond construction.

  TECHNIQUE 5 – ALTERNATE FURROW IRRIGATION:
  - Irrigate every alternate furrow in one irrigation.
  - Next irrigation, irrigate the remaining furrows.
  - Saves 25–30% water with less than 5% yield reduction.

  TECHNIQUE 6 – NIGHT IRRIGATION:
  - Irrigate during night time (9 PM to 6 AM) when evaporation is low.
  - Can save 15–20% water compared to afternoon irrigation.

  TECHNIQUE 7 – LASER LAND LEVELLING:
  - Level field precisely using laser-guided equipment.
  - Ensures uniform water distribution, eliminates waterlogged and
    dry patches, and saves 20–25% water per irrigation.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 8: PRACTICAL FARMER TIPS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  TIP 1 – KNOW YOUR SOIL TYPE FIRST:
  Sandy soils dry out faster and need more frequent but lighter
  irrigations. Black cotton soil (clay) holds water much longer and
  needs fewer, deeper irrigations. Adjust your irrigation schedule to
  match your soil, not your neighbor's practice.

  TIP 2 – USE THE FINGER TEST EVERY TIME:
  Before every irrigation, push your finger 5 cm into the soil between
  two rows in the middle of the field. If it feels dry, irrigate. If it
  still feels moist, wait 2–3 more days. This simple test saves thousands
  of litres of water every season.

  TIP 3 – NEVER IRRIGATE A WATERLOGGED FIELD:
  If you see standing water or very wet soil from previous rain, stop
  irrigation immediately — even if it is "irrigation day." Open drainage
  channels first. Irrigating a wet field drowns roots.

  TIP 4 – DO NOT SKIP THE FLOWERING IRRIGATION:
  Many farmers skip irrigation during flowering because the plant
  "looks okay." Flowers need water from inside the plant, not just
  visible wilting. Always ensure soil moisture is adequate from first
  flower opening to boll set. This one irrigation can save 40% of your
  potential yield.

  TIP 5 – APPLY DRIP IF POSSIBLE:
  If you have 2–3 acres or more of irrigated cotton, a drip system with
  government subsidy pays for itself in 2–3 seasons through water
  savings, fertilizer savings, and higher yield. Apply for PMKSY subsidy
  through your state agriculture department or online portal.

  TIP 6 – STOP IRRIGATION 3 WEEKS BEFORE FINAL PICKING:
  Final boll opening needs dry conditions. Wet bolls develop mould and
  the lint sticks together, reducing quality and market price. Stop all
  irrigation 20–25 days before the final cotton picking.

  TIP 7 – IRRIGATE IN THE EVENING DURING SUMMER:
  Morning and afternoon irrigations lose 20–30% of water to evaporation
  in hot weather (April–June). Evening and night irrigation is more
  efficient and keeps the soil cooler.

  TIP 8 – TRACK RAINFALL AND IRRIGATION TOGETHER:
  Keep a simple notebook with date, rainfall amount, and irrigation done.
  This helps you avoid over-irrigating after rain and ensures you never
  miss a critical irrigation. It takes only 2 minutes per week.

  TIP 9 – CLEAN DRIP FILTERS EVERY WEEK:
  If you use drip irrigation, a clogged filter or dripper can leave
  patches of your field dry. Clean the filter every week during the
  season. Flush the drip lines once a month with clean water. A
  functioning drip system is worth far more than a neglected one.

  TIP 10 – CONSULT KRUSHHIBANDHU FOR IRRIGATION ADVICE:
  Irrigation needs vary by soil type, variety, weather, and growth
  stage. Use the KrushiBandhu AI assistant for stage-specific, region-
  specific irrigation guidance in your local language, especially
  during unexpected drought or heavy rain conditions.
