SECTION 1: IMPORTANCE OF SOIL HEALTH
================================================================================

Soil is not just dirt under your feet. It is a living, breathing system that feeds
your crop, holds water, and supports the roots that keep your cotton plant standing.
Healthy soil is the foundation of a good cotton harvest. Without it, no amount of
fertilizer, water, or pesticide can fully save your crop.

Think of soil as a bank account. Every good practice you do — adding compost,
rotating crops, reducing tillage — deposits health into that account. Every bad
practice — overusing chemicals, leaving soil bare, over-irrigation — withdraws
from it. If you keep withdrawing without depositing, the account runs dry.

WHY SOIL HEALTH MATTERS FOR COTTON:

  ► Healthy soil provides nutrients that cotton needs to grow strong bolls and
    produce good quality fiber.
  ► Good soil structure allows roots to go deep, helping the plant survive dry
    periods and stand strong against winds.
  ► Healthy soil holds moisture better, reducing how often you need to irrigate.
  ► Soil with good microbial activity naturally suppresses some diseases and pests.
  ► Healthy soil reduces your input costs over time — you need less fertilizer,
    less water, and fewer pest treatments.
  ► Degraded soil produces lower yields year after year, no matter how much
    you spend on inputs.

A farmer who takes care of the soil takes care of the future of his farm.

SECTION 2: IDEAL SOIL FOR COTTON
================================================================================

Cotton is a strong and adaptable crop, but it grows best and produces the highest
yields in specific soil conditions. Knowing your ideal soil helps you understand
what improvements your field may need.

SOIL TYPE:
  - Cotton grows best in deep, well-drained loamy or clay-loam soils.
  - Black cotton soil (also called Regur or Vertisol) found across central and
    western India is naturally well-suited for cotton. It holds moisture well
    and is rich in calcium, magnesium, and potassium.
  - Sandy soils can also support cotton but need more irrigation and organic
    matter to retain nutrients.
  - Avoid very heavy clay soils that become waterlogged — cotton roots cannot
    breathe in waterlogged conditions and the plant will suffer.

SOIL DEPTH:
  - Cotton has a deep taproot that can go 1.5 to 2 meters into the soil.
  - Soil should be at least 60–90 cm deep for good root development.
  - Shallow, rocky, or hardpan soils restrict root growth and reduce yield.

SOIL pH (ACIDITY / ALKALINITY):
  - Cotton grows best at a pH of 6.0 to 8.0 (slightly acidic to slightly
    alkaline).
  - The ideal pH is 6.5 to 7.5.
  - pH below 6.0 (too acidic): Add agricultural lime to raise pH.
  - pH above 8.5 (too alkaline/saline): Add gypsum or sulphur to lower pH.
  - Always test pH before making adjustments.

DRAINAGE:
  - Cotton cannot tolerate waterlogging even for short periods.
  - Fields must have good natural or constructed drainage to remove excess
    water after heavy rains.
  - Provide drainage channels or raised bed planting in flood-prone areas.

SOIL TEXTURE SUMMARY:

  Soil Type         | Suitability for Cotton | Main Challenge
  ----------------- | ---------------------- | --------------------------
  Black cotton soil | Excellent              | Needs drainage in wet years
  Clay-loam         | Very Good              | Avoid waterlogging
  Loamy             | Good                   | Needs regular organic matter
  Sandy loam        | Fair                   | Needs more irrigation
  Sandy             | Poor                   | High water and nutrient loss
  Heavy clay        | Poor                   | Waterlogging, root stress

SECTION 3: SOIL TESTING
================================================================================

Soil testing is like a health check-up for your field. It tells you exactly what
nutrients are present, what is missing, and what corrections need to be made.
Many farmers apply fertilizers blindly — too much of one nutrient and too little
of another. This wastes money and can even damage the crop. Soil testing removes
this guesswork.

WHEN TO TEST:
  - Test your soil at least ONCE every 2–3 years.
  - Best time to test: 4–6 weeks before planting cotton, after the previous
    crop has been harvested and field is ploughed.
  - If you see unusual symptoms in your crop (yellowing, stunted growth,
    poor boll development), test soil immediately.

HOW TO COLLECT A SOIL SAMPLE:
  Step 1: Divide your field into sections if different parts look or perform
          differently.
  Step 2: In each section, use a clean spade or soil auger to collect soil
          from 10–15 random spots in a zigzag pattern.
  Step 3: Collect soil from a depth of 0–15 cm (for nutrient analysis) and
          also 15–30 cm (for sub-soil assessment if needed).
  Step 4: Mix all the samples from one section together in a clean bucket.
  Step 5: Take about 500 grams of the mixed soil, dry it in shade, and pack
          in a clean cloth bag or plastic bag.
  Step 6: Label the bag with your name, field location, and crop name.
  Step 7: Submit the sample to your nearest Soil Testing Laboratory (STL),
          Krishi Vigyan Kendra (KVK), or agriculture department office.

WHAT SOIL TESTING TELLS YOU:
  - pH level (acidity or alkalinity)
  - Organic carbon content
  - Available Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K)
  - Secondary nutrients: Calcium (Ca), Magnesium (Mg), Sulphur (S)
  - Micronutrients: Zinc (Zn), Boron (B), Iron (Fe), Manganese (Mn)
  - Salinity and electrical conductivity (important in Gujarat and Rajasthan)

GOVERNMENT SOIL HEALTH CARD SCHEME:
  Under the Government of India's Soil Health Card (SHC) scheme, farmers can get
  free soil testing done at government labs. The card gives fertilizer
  recommendations specific to your soil and crop. Contact your local agriculture
  extension officer to apply.

SECTION 4: IMPROVING SOIL FERTILITY
================================================================================

Soil fertility means how well your soil can supply nutrients to the cotton plant.
A fertile soil does not just have nutrients — it also has the physical structure
and biological life to make those nutrients available to plant roots.

BALANCED FERTILIZER USE (Based on Soil Test Recommendations):

  Cotton generally needs these nutrients per acre (adjust based on soil test):

  Nutrient          | Recommended Dose (per acre)  | Source
  ----------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------
  Nitrogen (N)      | 40–60 kg                      | Urea, DAP, FYM
  Phosphorus (P)    | 20–30 kg                      | Single Superphosphate, DAP
  Potassium (K)     | 20–30 kg                      | Muriate of Potash (MOP)
  Sulphur (S)       | 10–15 kg                      | Gypsum, SSP
  Zinc (Zn)         | 5 kg (if deficient)           | Zinc Sulphate

  NITROGEN APPLICATION TIPS:
  - Do not apply all nitrogen at once. Split it into 2–3 doses.
  - Apply 1/3 at planting, 1/3 at squaring (flower bud stage), and 1/3 at
    boll development.
  - This reduces wastage and gives a steady supply to the plant.

  PHOSPHORUS AND POTASSIUM:
  - Apply the full dose at or before planting as a basal dose.
  - Incorporate into the soil rather than leaving on the surface.

MICRONUTRIENT DEFICIENCY SIGNS IN COTTON:

  Deficiency      | Visible Symptom
  --------------- | --------------------------------------------------
  Zinc (Zn)       | Young leaves small, bronze, and distorted
  Boron (B)       | Terminal buds die, bolls rough and deformed
  Iron (Fe)       | Young leaves turn pale yellow between green veins
  Magnesium (Mg)  | Older leaves turn yellow, veins stay green
  Sulphur (S)     | Young leaves yellow, plant growth slows

  If you see these signs, get a soil or leaf test done and apply the specific
  micronutrient as recommended. Do not guess — applying the wrong nutrient wastes
  money and can make things worse.

SECTION 5: ORGANIC MATTER MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Organic matter is the life force of healthy soil. It comes from decomposed plant
and animal material — compost, farmyard manure, crop residues, and green manures.
Organic matter feeds soil microorganisms, improves soil structure, retains
moisture, and slowly releases nutrients to your cotton crop.

WHY ORGANIC MATTER IS CRITICAL:
  - Improves soil texture — makes clay soils less sticky and sandy soils
    less loose.
  - Increases water holding capacity — reduces irrigation needs.
  - Feeds billions of beneficial soil microbes that release nutrients for the plant.
  - Buffers soil pH changes.
  - Reduces the amount of chemical fertilizer needed over time.

HOW TO ADD ORGANIC MATTER:

  ► FARMYARD MANURE (FYM):
    Apply 4–6 tonnes of well-composted farmyard manure per acre, 3–4 weeks
    before planting. Incorporate into the soil by ploughing.
    FYM improves soil structure and provides slow-release nutrients.

  ► VERMICOMPOST:
    Apply 1–2 tonnes of vermicompost per acre as a basal dose.
    Vermicompost is richer in nutrients than regular FYM and also introduces
    beneficial microorganisms into the soil.

  ► GREEN MANURING:
    Grow a fast-growing green manure crop like Dhaincha (Sesbania bispinosa)
    or Sun Hemp (Crotalaria juncea) before cotton.
    When the crop is 45–60 days old, plough it into the soil.
    This adds fresh organic matter and nitrogen directly to the soil —
    completely free and natural.
    Green manuring can substitute for 20–30 kg/acre of urea.

  ► CROP RESIDUE INCORPORATION:
    Instead of burning cotton stalks after harvest, chop them and plough
    them into the soil. They slowly decompose and add organic matter.
    Stalk burning destroys organic matter, kills soil organisms, and releases
    air pollution. Avoid burning whenever possible.

  ► COMPOST:
    Make your own compost from kitchen waste, dry leaves, weeds, and animal
    dung. A compost pit takes 60–90 days to produce ready compost.
    Apply 2–3 tonnes per acre before planting.

TARGET ORGANIC CARBON LEVEL:
  Healthy cotton soil should have at least 0.5–0.75% organic carbon content.
  Many Indian cotton soils are below 0.5% due to years of chemical farming.
  Building organic carbon is a long-term process — be patient and consistent.

SECTION 6: SOIL MOISTURE MANAGEMENT
================================================================================

Cotton needs adequate and steady moisture — but not too much, not too little.
Managing soil moisture correctly protects the crop during dry spells, reduces
waterlogging stress, and reduces your water and irrigation costs.

CRITICAL STAGES FOR MOISTURE:

  Growth Stage           | Moisture Importance
  ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------
  Germination (0–10 days)| Adequate moisture for uniform germination
  Squaring (50–70 days)  | Moisture stress causes flower bud drop
  Flowering (70–90 days) | Most critical — stress causes flower and boll drop
  Boll development       | Steady moisture for fiber development
  Boll opening           | Reduce moisture to avoid boll rot and disease

IRRIGATION TIPS:
  - Irrigate based on soil moisture and crop stage, not a fixed calendar.
  - Use the "finger test": push your finger 5 cm into the soil. If it feels
    dry, it is time to irrigate. If moist, wait another 2–3 days.
  - Flood irrigation wastes water and can cause waterlogging. Where possible,
    use:
    - Furrow irrigation: water flows between plant rows, not directly on plants.
    - Drip irrigation: delivers water directly to the root zone. Saves 40–60%
      water compared to flood irrigation and reduces soil erosion and disease.

MULCHING:
  - Cover the soil between cotton rows with dry straw, sugarcane bagasse, or
    dry leaves after planting.
  - A 5–8 cm layer of mulch reduces water evaporation from the soil by up to
    50%, keeps soil temperature stable, and suppresses weed growth.
  - Mulch also slowly decomposes and adds organic matter to the soil.

AVOIDING WATERLOGGING:
  - Create drainage channels along field borders before the monsoon season.
  - Raised bed planting (planting cotton on raised ridges) improves drainage
    and reduces waterlogging risk in heavy clay soils.
  - Never irrigate just before a heavy rain is expected.

SECTION 7: PREVENTING SOIL EROSION
================================================================================

Soil erosion means the top layer of your soil is being washed away or blown away.
The top 15–20 cm of soil is the most fertile layer — it contains most of the
organic matter and nutrients. Once this layer is gone, it takes decades to rebuild.
Erosion is a serious, silent problem in many cotton-growing areas.

CAUSES OF SOIL EROSION IN COTTON FIELDS:
  - Heavy rain falling on bare, open soil.
  - Overwatering with flood irrigation.
  - Steep slopes without protective plants or barriers.
  - Leaving the field bare after harvest with no cover crop or mulch.
  - Burning crop residues removes the protective cover.

HOW TO PREVENT EROSION:

  ► COVER CROPS:
    After cotton harvest, sow a cover crop like cowpea, moong bean, or
    wheat. The roots hold soil in place and the canopy protects from rain.

  ► CONTOUR FARMING:
    On sloped land, plough and plant in lines across the slope (not up and
    down). This slows down water flow and reduces soil washing.

  ► BUNDS AND FIELD BORDERS:
    Build soil bunds (raised ridges) around field borders to hold water
    inside the field and prevent runoff from carrying soil away.

  ► MULCHING:
    As described above, mulching protects the soil surface from direct
    raindrop impact, which is the main cause of surface erosion.

  ► WINDBREAKS:
    Plant rows of trees like Subabool, Neem, or Eucalyptus on the western
    or northern borders of large fields to reduce wind erosion.

  ► MINIMUM TILLAGE:
    Excessive ploughing breaks down soil structure and increases erosion risk.
    Reduce deep tillage where possible.

SECTION 8: CROP ROTATION FOR SOIL HEALTH
================================================================================

Crop rotation means growing different crops one after another in the same field,
in a planned sequence, across different seasons or years. It is one of the most
powerful and completely free tools for improving soil health.

WHY CROP ROTATION IS ESSENTIAL:

  ► BREAKS PEST AND DISEASE CYCLES:
    Many cotton pests and soil-borne pathogens survive in the soil or on crop
    residues. Growing a different crop breaks their cycle — they find no
    suitable host and their population collapses.

  ► RESTORES SOIL NUTRIENTS:
    Legume crops (chickpea, groundnut, moong, soybean) fix nitrogen from the
    air into the soil through their roots. After harvesting a legume crop,
    the field is naturally enriched with 20–40 kg of nitrogen per acre —
    completely free.

  ► IMPROVES SOIL STRUCTURE:
    Different crops have different root systems. Deep-rooted crops break up
    hardpan layers. Fibrous-rooted crops add organic matter near the surface.

  ► REDUCES WEED PRESSURE:
    Different crops allow different weed management methods, reducing the
    population of weeds that are hard to control in cotton.

RECOMMENDED ROTATION SEQUENCES FOR COTTON FARMERS:

  Sequence                        | Benefit
  ------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------
  Cotton → Wheat → Chickpea       | Classic rotation for central India
  Cotton → Groundnut → Sorghum    | Good for Gujarat and Maharashtra
  Cotton → Moong Bean → Wheat     | Short cycle with N-fixation
  Cotton → Sunflower → Chickpea   | Diversified income + soil benefit
  Cotton → Fallow (Green Manure)  | Intensive soil restoration approach

IMPORTANT RULE: Never grow cotton in the same field for more than 2 years in a row.
After 2 years of cotton, rotate with at least one season of a non-cotton crop.

SECTION 9: SUSTAINABLE SOIL PRACTICES
================================================================================

Sustainable soil management means taking care of your soil today so that it remains
productive for your children and grandchildren. Here are the most important
long-term practices every cotton farmer should follow:

  ► REDUCE CHEMICAL DEPENDENCY GRADUALLY:
    Slowly reduce dependence on synthetic fertilizers by increasing the use of
    compost, FYM, green manures, and biofertilizers.
    This does not mean stopping chemicals overnight — it means slowly replacing
    a portion of chemical inputs with organic alternatives each year.

  ► USE BIOFERTILIZERS:
    - Rhizobium: Fixes atmospheric nitrogen. Use for legume crops in rotation.
    - Azospirillum: Fixes nitrogen for cotton. Apply as seed treatment.
    - Phosphate Solubilizing Bacteria (PSB): Makes locked phosphorus available
      to plant roots. Apply as seed treatment or soil drench.
    - These are available at KVKs and agriculture supply shops.
      Biofertilizers are low-cost and reduce the need for chemical fertilizers.

  ► MINIMIZE BURNING OF CROP RESIDUES:
    Burning destroys organic matter, kills beneficial soil organisms, and
    releases carbon dioxide and pollutants. Chop and incorporate residues
    into soil instead. Over 3–5 years, this dramatically improves soil health.

  ► PRACTICE MINIMUM TILLAGE WHERE POSSIBLE:
    Deep ploughing every year destroys soil structure and kills earthworms
    and beneficial microbes. After the initial deep ploughing to establish
    the field, reduce tillage depth in subsequent years.

  ► MAINTAIN SOIL COVER AT ALL TIMES:
    Never leave soil bare for long periods. After harvesting cotton, sow a
    cover crop or apply mulch immediately. Bare soil loses moisture, loses
    nutrients, and is vulnerable to erosion.

  ► PROTECT AND ENCOURAGE EARTHWORMS:
    Earthworms are nature's soil engineers. They aerate soil, improve
    drainage, and convert organic matter into rich vermicompost. Reducing
    chemical pesticides and increasing organic matter encourages earthworms.
    A healthy field should have at least 5–10 earthworms per square foot of
    soil when dug to 15 cm depth.

  ► MONITOR AND REACT:
    Get your soil tested every 2–3 years. Compare results over time. If
    organic carbon is increasing and pH is stable, your practices are working.
    If the soil is degrading, change your approach before it is too late.

================================================================================

Before Planting:
  □ Soil test done in last 2 years? If not, collect and send a sample.
  □ pH between 6.0–8.0? Correct if outside this range.

  □ Irrigation managed based on soil moisture, not a fixed schedule?
  □ Mulch applied between rows?
  □ Fertilizers split-applied as per schedule?
  □ Weeds removed on time (no competition for nutrients)?

After Harvest:
  □ Crop residues chopped and incorporated (not burned)?
  □ Cover crop sown to protect soil?
  □ Crop rotation plan ready for next season?
  □ Farm diary updated with observations for the season?
