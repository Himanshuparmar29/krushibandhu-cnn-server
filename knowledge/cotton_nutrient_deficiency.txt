INTRODUCTION
------------
A healthy cotton plant needs a balanced supply of nutrients at every stage
of growth. When any nutrient is missing or insufficient in the soil, the
plant shows visible warning signs — on leaves, stems, bolls, or in its
overall growth pattern. These warning signs are the plant's way of telling
the farmer that something is wrong.

Recognizing nutrient deficiency symptoms early and correcting them quickly
can prevent serious yield loss. This guide explains the six most common
nutrient deficiencies in cotton — what they look like, why they happen,
and how to fix them.

GOLDEN RULE FOR DIAGNOSIS:
- Deficiency in OLDER (lower) leaves first = Mobile nutrients
  (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium, Magnesium).
  The plant moves these nutrients from old leaves to new growing parts.
- Deficiency in YOUNGER (upper/new) leaves first = Immobile nutrients
  (Zinc, Iron, Boron, Calcium).
  The plant cannot move these from old leaves to new growth.
This rule helps you identify the deficient nutrient faster.

================================================================================
NUTRIENT 1: NITROGEN (N)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF NITROGEN
--------------------
Nitrogen is the most important nutrient for cotton growth. It is the
building block of proteins, enzymes, and chlorophyll — the green pigment
that allows the plant to make food from sunlight. Nitrogen drives all
vegetative growth: leaves, stems, and branches. It directly determines
how many fruiting branches the plant develops and how many bolls it can
support. Without adequate nitrogen, a cotton plant stays small, pale,
and produces far fewer bolls than its potential.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Yellowing (chlorosis) starts on the OLDER, LOWER leaves
  first. This is because nitrogen is a mobile nutrient — the plant
  pulls nitrogen from old leaves and sends it to new growing parts.
- Leaves turn pale green first, then bright yellow, then light brown
  and dry from the tip and margins inward.
- Veins also turn yellow (entire leaf is uniformly pale — no green
  patches or patterns).
- The whole plant looks light green, stunted, and thin compared to
  a healthy plant.
- Stem is thin and weak. Fewer branches develop.
- Fewer squares (flower buds) form, and existing squares may shed.
- In severe cases, lower leaves dry up completely and fall off.
- Overall: The plant looks washed out, pale, and low-energy.

3. CAUSES
----------
- Soil naturally low in nitrogen (sandy soils, light-colored soils).
- Not applying enough nitrogen fertilizer or skipping a top dressing.
- Applying all nitrogen at sowing only — most of it leaches away before
  the plant can use it.
- Heavy or continuous rainfall that washes nitrogen (leaching) out of
  the root zone.
- Waterlogged soil where nitrogen converts to gas and escapes
  (denitrification).
- Soil organic matter very low — less natural nitrogen release.
- Previous crop was also a heavy nitrogen feeder (e.g., maize, sugarcane)
  and left the soil depleted.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
IMMEDIATE (SOIL APPLICATION):
- Apply Urea @ 15–20 kg per acre as a top dressing immediately.
- Broadcast between the rows and irrigate lightly within 24 hours to
  dissolve urea into the root zone.
- Do not place urea directly against the stem — it can burn roots.

QUICK EMERGENCY (FOLIAR SPRAY):
- Spray 2% Urea solution on leaves (dissolve 20 g of urea in 1 litre
  of water) — for rapid recovery within 5–7 days.
- Spray in the early morning or late evening. Avoid spraying in hot
  afternoon sun — leaf burn can occur.
- Repeat foliar spray after 10 days if yellowing persists.

PREVENTIVE (FOR NEXT SEASON):
- Follow the 3-split nitrogen schedule: 1/3 at sowing, 1/3 at 30 DAS,
  1/3 at 60 DAS.
- Add FYM or compost (4–5 tonnes per acre) to improve soil nitrogen
  levels naturally over time.
- Use biofertilizer Azospirillum at sowing to boost nitrogen availability.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Peele patte = Naitrajan ki kami" — Yellow leaves starting from the
  bottom always indicate nitrogen shortage in cotton.
- Never apply all urea at sowing. Splitting saves money and gives better
  results — 30–40% more efficient nitrogen use.
- Always irrigate the field within 24 hours of broadcasting urea. Urea
  left on dry soil loses nitrogen as gas into the air within 2–3 days.
- If the whole plant is uniformly pale from seedling stage onward, the
  soil is very poor in nitrogen. Apply basal FYM every season.
- Foliar urea spray gives faster visual results than soil application —
  use it when you need quick recovery during flowering or boll stage.

================================================================================
NUTRIENT 2: PHOSPHORUS (P)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF PHOSPHORUS
----------------------
Phosphorus is the foundation nutrient for root development and early plant
establishment. It is essential for energy transfer within the plant —
every process that requires energy (germination, root growth, flowering,
seed filling) depends on phosphorus. In cotton, it supports deep root
growth, strong seedling establishment, timely flowering, and good seed
development inside the boll. Plants with good phosphorus availability
are more drought-tolerant because their deep, strong roots can access
water and nutrients from deeper soil layers.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Older, lower leaves show a distinctive dark green to
  purple-red or reddish-bronze discoloration — especially on the
  underside of leaves and on the stems and petioles (leaf stalks).
- This purplish color is caused by accumulation of anthocyanin pigment
  when phosphorus is insufficient.
- Leaves may appear dark green initially (the plant holds excess
  chlorophyll trying to compensate), then turn purple-bronze.
- Root system is shallow and poorly developed — plants can be pulled
  out of the soil very easily.
- Plant growth is slow and stunted. Stem remains thin.
- Flowering is delayed by 7–14 days compared to healthy plants.
- Bolls are fewer and smaller. Seed development inside bolls is poor.
- In severe cases, leaves may turn brown and drop prematurely.

3. CAUSES
----------
- Soil pH below 5.5 or above 8.5 — phosphorus becomes "locked" in
  the soil and unavailable to the plant even if applied.
- Sandy soils with very low phosphorus levels naturally.
- Phosphorus fertilizer not applied or applied in insufficient quantity.
- Cold soil temperature (below 15°C) slows phosphorus uptake by roots.
- Poor root development from other stresses (waterlogging, root pests)
  that limit the plant's ability to absorb phosphorus.
- Phosphorus fertilizer applied on the soil surface without incorporation
  — it does not move in the soil and stays out of reach of roots.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
PREVENTIVE (BEST APPROACH — At sowing):
- Apply full phosphorus dose at sowing time, mixed into the soil in the
  root zone (10–15 cm depth).
- SSP (Single Super Phosphate) @ 125 kg per acre — best for cotton as
  it also provides Sulphur.
- OR DAP @ 50 kg per acre for a concentrated phosphorus source.

SOIL pH CORRECTION:
- If soil pH is too high (above 8.0): Apply Gypsum @ 200 kg per acre
  or Sulphur @ 20–25 kg per acre to reduce pH gradually.
- If soil pH is too low (below 6.0): Apply agricultural lime as per
  soil test recommendation.

FOLIAR SPRAY (for quick correction in standing crop):
- Spray 2% Di-Ammonium Phosphate (DAP) solution: Dissolve 20 g DAP
  in 1 litre of water and spray on leaves.
- Repeat every 10–15 days until symptoms reduce.

BIOLOGICAL APPROACH:
- Apply Phosphate Solubilizing Bacteria (PSB) @ 2.5 kg per acre in
  soil at sowing — PSB unlocks soil phosphorus and makes it available.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Baingani ya lal patte = Phosphorus ki kami" — Purple-red coloring
  on older leaves and stems is the key sign of phosphorus deficiency.
- Phosphorus does NOT move in the soil. If you apply SSP or DAP on the
  soil surface and do not mix it in, the roots cannot reach it. Always
  incorporate phosphorus into the soil at sowing.
- A soil test is essential because phosphorus deficiency is often caused
  by wrong soil pH, not just low phosphorus levels. Fixing pH will
  automatically fix phosphorus availability.
- SSP is a better choice than DAP in many cotton soils because it also
  provides Sulphur — an often-neglected nutrient that cotton needs.
- Phosphorus deficiency that starts early (seedling stage) cannot be
  fully corrected mid-season. Prevention at sowing time is essential.

NUTRIENT 3: POTASSIUM (K)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF POTASSIUM
---------------------
Potassium is called the "quality nutrient" in cotton because of its
direct impact on lint quality — fiber length, strength, and fineness.
It regulates water movement inside plant cells, helping the plant handle
drought and heat stress. Potassium strengthens cell walls, making plants
more resistant to fungal diseases and pest attack. It activates over 60
plant enzymes and is critical for boll development, uniform boll opening,
and movement of sugars from leaves to bolls (boll filling). A potassium-
deficient plant produces fewer, lighter bolls with weaker, shorter fiber.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Leaf margins (the edges/borders of leaves) turn yellow,
  then brown and dry — a symptom called "marginal leaf scorch" or
  "leaf margin burn."
- Starts on the OLDER, LOWER leaves first (potassium is mobile).
- The burnt, brown margin is sharp and clearly defined, while the
  center of the leaf stays green longer.
- As deficiency worsens, the browning moves inward from the margins.
- Leaves may curl upward or downward.
- In severe cases, entire leaves turn brown and fall off, starting from
  the bottom of the plant upward.
- Plants wilt quickly under heat or mild drought — because potassium is
  needed for water regulation in cells.
- Bolls are small, lightweight, and do not open fully or uniformly.
- Lint is short, weak, and of poor market quality.
- A well-known and severe form called "Cotton Boll Shedding" is often
  linked to potassium deficiency at boll development stage.

3. CAUSES
----------
- Sandy soils are naturally very low in potassium (it leaches easily).
- Farmers in India traditionally apply N and P but often skip potassium.
- Continuous cotton cultivation without rotation depletes soil potassium
  over many seasons.
- Heavy rainfall or excess irrigation causes potassium leaching in
  light soils.
- High calcium or magnesium in soil competes with potassium for
  root absorption.
- Soil pH extremes (very acidic or very alkaline) reduce potassium
  availability.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
SOIL APPLICATION (Recommended):
- Apply Muriate of Potash (MOP) @ 33 kg per acre at sowing as part
  of basal dose.
- OR Sulphate of Potash (SOP) @ 27 kg per acre (preferred for
  high-quality lint production — also provides sulphur).

FOLIAR SPRAY (For quick correction and boll filling):
- Spray 1% Potassium Nitrate (KNO3) solution: Dissolve 10 g in 1 litre
  of water. Spray at squaring, flowering, and boll development stages.
- OR spray 0.5% Potassium Sulphate (K2SO4) solution.
- Foliar potassium spray at boll development stage is one of the most
  cost-effective yield-boosting practices in cotton.

FERTIGATION (Drip users):
- Apply SOP or KNO3 through drip system at 2–3 kg per acre per week
  during flowering and boll development stage.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Patti ke kinarey jalana = Potash ki kami" — Burnt or scorched leaf
  margins starting from older leaves is the classic sign of potassium
  deficiency. Easy to identify once you have seen it once.
- Potassium is directly connected to your cotton's market price. Better
  potassium = longer, stronger fiber = higher grade = better price.
  Do not skip potassium to save money — it costs you more at the market.
- Spray KNO3 at 1% on leaves during boll development even if no symptoms
  are visible — it is a proven yield and quality booster.
- If you are in a drought year, potassium nutrition is extra critical.
  Plants with good potassium handle water stress far better.
- In sandy fields or fields with a history of heavy leaching, apply
  potassium in 2 splits: half at sowing and half at 45 DAS.

================================================================================
NUTRIENT 4: MAGNESIUM (Mg)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF MAGNESIUM
---------------------
Magnesium is the central atom of the chlorophyll molecule — the green
pigment in leaves that captures sunlight and drives photosynthesis. Without
magnesium, the plant cannot make enough chlorophyll and therefore cannot
produce enough energy to grow, flower, and fill bolls. Magnesium also
helps in the movement of phosphorus within the plant and plays a role in
enzyme activation. In cotton, magnesium deficiency is increasingly common
in intensively farmed soils with high potassium and calcium inputs, which
compete with magnesium for absorption by roots.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Yellowing (chlorosis) begins BETWEEN the veins of the
  leaf. The veins themselves stay green while the areas between them
  turn yellow — this distinctive pattern is called "interveinal
  chlorosis."
- Starts on OLDER, LOWER leaves first (magnesium is mobile).
- Initially the leaf looks like a green net with yellow patches between
  the veins. As deficiency worsens, more of the leaf turns yellow.
- Leaf edges may turn brown and dry in severe cases.
- In very severe deficiency, the entire older leaf turns yellow with
  only the main veins remaining green.
- Young leaves at the top remain green and normal in appearance (because
  the plant moves magnesium from old leaves to new growth).
- Bolls are lighter and fill poorly. Lint quality is reduced.
- Plants are less efficient at photosynthesis — growth slows and plant
  looks generally less vigorous.

  KEY DISTINCTION:
  - Magnesium deficiency: Interveinal chlorosis on OLDER leaves,
    veins remain green.
  - Iron deficiency: Interveinal chlorosis on YOUNGER (new) leaves,
    veins remain green.
  This distinction helps identify which nutrient is deficient.

3. CAUSES
----------
- Sandy soils and highly leached soils are naturally low in magnesium.
- Continuous use of high-potassium and high-calcium fertilizers
  reduces magnesium absorption by roots (nutrient competition).
- Acidic soils (pH below 5.5) lose magnesium through leaching.
- Soils with very high calcium (calcareous soils, pH above 8.0) block
  magnesium uptake.
- Lack of organic matter in soil reduces natural magnesium availability.
- Magnesium fertilizers are rarely applied by farmers — it is often a
  forgotten secondary nutrient.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
SOIL APPLICATION:
- Apply Magnesium Sulphate (MgSO4 / Epsom Salt) @ 10–15 kg per acre
  in soil before sowing or as a top dressing.
- Dolomite limestone @ 100–200 kg per acre — provides both magnesium
  and calcium and also improves acidic soil pH.

FOLIAR SPRAY (Fastest method for correction):
- Spray 2% Magnesium Sulphate (Epsom Salt) solution: Dissolve 20 g
  in 1 litre of water.
- Apply to leaves in the early morning or evening.
- Repeat every 10–15 days until interveinal yellowing disappears.
- Foliar spray gives visual recovery within 7–10 days.

ORGANIC APPROACH:
- Regular application of FYM and compost helps maintain magnesium
  levels in soil naturally over multiple seasons.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Nasdaar peela patta = Magnesium ki kami" — Yellowing between veins
  (veins stay green) on lower leaves is the signature sign of magnesium
  deficiency. It looks like a yellow leaf with a green net pattern.
- Magnesium Sulphate (Epsom Salt) is cheap, widely available, and
  dissolves easily in water for foliar spray. Keep a small stock
  available for quick correction during the season.
- If you are using a lot of MOP (Muriate of Potash) every season,
  you may be causing magnesium deficiency by competing nutrients.
  Consider switching partly to SOP (Sulphate of Potash) which does
  not compete as aggressively.
- Magnesium deficiency is often misdiagnosed as nitrogen deficiency
  because both cause yellowing. The key difference: nitrogen deficiency
  causes uniform yellowing; magnesium causes interveinal yellowing with
  green veins remaining.
- Adding dolomite as your liming material (instead of regular lime)
  gives you both calcium and magnesium in one application.

NUTRIENT 5: ZINC (Zn)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF ZINC
----------------
Zinc is a micronutrient — needed in very small quantities — but it plays
a big role in cotton productivity. Zinc is required for the production of
auxins (plant growth hormones) that control stem elongation and leaf
expansion. It is also essential for enzyme functions involved in protein
synthesis, carbohydrate metabolism, and chlorophyll formation. Zinc
deficiency is the most common micronutrient deficiency in Indian cotton
soils and can reduce yield by 15–25% even when macronutrient nutrition
is adequate. Most Indian cotton soils — especially calcareous (alkaline)
black soils — are zinc-deficient.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Appears on NEW, YOUNG leaves first (zinc is immobile —
  the plant cannot move it from old to new tissues).
- Young leaves are noticeably smaller than normal — a hallmark sign.
- Leaves show a bronze, rust-brown, or copper-colored discoloration,
  especially on the upper surface.
- Interveinal chlorosis (yellowing between veins) on young leaves —
  similar to iron deficiency but with bronze/brown tones rather than
  bright yellow.
- Shortened internodes — the stem segments between leaves are shorter
  than normal. The plant looks compact and stunted with a "rosette"
  appearance (leaves bunched together at the top).
- Leaf surface may show small, irregular brown necrotic spots.
- New leaves may be thick, leathery, and distorted in severe cases.
- Flowering is delayed. Fewer squares form.
- Bolls are smaller and fiber quality is poor.

3. CAUSES
----------
- Most common in calcareous soils (high pH, above 7.5) where zinc
  is chemically locked and unavailable to roots.
- Sandy soils with low organic matter.
- Soils that have been intensively cultivated for many years without
  micronutrient replenishment.
- Excessive phosphorus application in previous seasons can reduce zinc
  availability (phosphorus-zinc antagonism).
- Flooded or waterlogged conditions temporarily lock zinc.
- Cold, wet weather at the start of the season reduces zinc uptake
  by roots.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
SOIL APPLICATION (Recommended every 2–3 years):
- Apply Zinc Sulphate (ZnSO4·7H2O, 21% Zn) @ 10 kg per acre in soil
  during land preparation. Mix well into the soil.
- Effect lasts 2–3 years in the soil. Repeat every 2–3 seasons.

FOLIAR SPRAY (For quick correction in standing crop):
- Spray 0.5% Zinc Sulphate solution: Dissolve 5 g of Zinc Sulphate
  in 1 litre of water.
- Add a small amount of lime (0.25 g per litre) or urea (5 g per litre)
  to prevent leaf burn.
- Spray at 30–35 DAS and again at squaring stage (45–55 DAS).
- Repeat every 15 days if symptoms persist.

SEED TREATMENT:
- Coat seeds with Zinc Sulphate @ 2 g per kg of seed before sowing.
  This provides a zinc boost at the most sensitive germination stage.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Chote patte aur bronze rang = Zinc ki kami" — Small, bronze-colored
  new leaves and a stunted, bushy-looking plant is the classic zinc
  deficiency sign in cotton.
- Zinc Sulphate soil application needs to be done only once every 2–3
  years — it is not needed every season. Keep records of when you
  last applied it.
- Zinc and phosphorus compete in the soil. If you apply very high
  phosphorus doses every year, you are likely creating a zinc
  deficiency — even if zinc is present in the soil.
- Zinc deficiency symptoms appear very early in the season (25–35 DAS).
  Early correction at this stage prevents yield loss. If you wait for
  boll stage to correct zinc, much of the yield loss has already happened.
- A soil test for micronutrients every 3 years is the best way to track
  zinc status in your field and apply it only when needed.

NUTRIENT 6: IRON (Fe)
================================================================================

1. ROLE OF IRON
----------------
Iron is essential for chlorophyll synthesis — without iron, the plant
cannot produce the green pigment that captures sunlight. Iron is also
required for many enzyme systems in the plant, especially those involved
in energy production and nitrogen fixation in the roots. While cotton does
not need large amounts of iron, the right quantity at the right time is
critical for maintaining dark green, photosynthetically active leaves.
Iron deficiency is particularly common in alkaline and calcareous soils —
the type of soils widely found in Gujarat, Rajasthan, and parts of
Maharashtra and Punjab where cotton is grown.

2. DEFICIENCY SYMPTOMS
-----------------------
- FIRST SIGN: Appears on the YOUNGEST, NEWEST leaves at the top of
  the plant first — because iron is immobile and cannot be moved from
  old leaves to new growth.
- Classic symptom: Bright yellow to almost white coloration between
  the leaf veins, while the veins themselves remain distinctly green —
  called "interveinal chlorosis on young leaves."
- In mild deficiency: Only the youngest leaves look yellow-green with
  a faint green vein network.
- In moderate deficiency: Leaves become bright yellow with clearly
  visible green veins — a striking, distinctive pattern.
- In severe deficiency: Entire new leaves turn pale yellow to white.
  The green vein network disappears as veins also lose color.
- The lower, older leaves remain green and appear normal (opposite of
  nitrogen deficiency).
- Plant growth slows. New shoot development is weak.
- Bolls do not develop fully. Early boll set is poor.

  KEY DISTINCTION FROM MAGNESIUM:
  - Iron: Interveinal chlorosis on NEW (young, top) leaves.
  - Magnesium: Interveinal chlorosis on OLD (lower) leaves.
  Both show green veins with yellow patches, but on different parts of
  the plant. This is the critical difference for correct diagnosis.

3. CAUSES
----------
- High soil pH (above 7.5–8.0) — the most common cause. In alkaline
  soils, iron converts to insoluble forms that roots cannot absorb.
  Iron may be physically present in the soil but chemically unavailable.
- Waterlogged or poorly drained soils where oxygen is low.
- High levels of calcium, phosphorus, or bicarbonates in the soil
  that compete with or block iron absorption.
- Cool soil temperatures in early season reduce iron uptake.
- Calcareous soils (soils with free calcium carbonate / lime content)
  — very common in Gujarat and Rajasthan cotton belt.
- Excessive phosphorus fertilization can induce iron deficiency.

4. CORRECTION METHODS
----------------------
FOLIAR SPRAY (Most effective method for iron):
- Spray 0.5% Ferrous Sulphate (FeSO4) solution: Dissolve 5 g of
  Ferrous Sulphate in 1 litre of water.
- Add a few drops of citric acid or 0.1% citric acid to prevent the
  iron from oxidizing and becoming inactive in the spray solution.
- Spray on young leaves (top of the plant) in the early morning.
- Repeat every 7–10 days until new leaves show normal dark green color.
- Foliar spray is preferred over soil application because soil iron is
  rapidly locked in alkaline conditions — foliar delivery bypasses
  the soil problem entirely.

CHELATED IRON (Most efficient but costlier):
- Apply Fe-EDTA (chelated iron) @ 1 g per litre as foliar spray.
- Chelated forms remain soluble and available for a longer period than
  plain ferrous sulphate.
- Recommended for severe or recurring iron deficiency.

SOIL pH CORRECTION (Long-term solution):
- Apply elemental Sulphur @ 20–25 kg per acre to gradually lower pH
  in alkaline soils over 1–2 seasons.
- Add organic matter (FYM, vermicompost) regularly — organic acids
  produced by decomposition help release locked iron in soil.

AVOID:
- Do not mix iron compounds with phosphate fertilizers in the same
  spray solution — they react and become inactive.

5. FARMER TIPS
---------------
- "Nayi pattiyaan peeli, nasdaar = Iron ki kami" — Bright yellow new
  leaves with clearly green veins at the top of the plant means iron
  deficiency. The older leaves look fine — that is the key clue.
- Iron deficiency in cotton is usually a SOIL pH PROBLEM, not a low
  iron problem. The iron is already in your soil but is locked by
  high pH. Foliar spray corrects the symptom quickly. Long-term
  correction needs pH management.
- Ferrous Sulphate spray is very cheap and effective. Buy it from any
  agri-input shop and keep it available. Early spraying at first sign
  of yellowing on new leaves prevents major yield loss.
- In areas with consistently high soil pH (above 8.0), apply Gypsum
  @ 200 kg per acre or Sulphur @ 20 kg per acre every 2–3 years as
  a long-term pH management strategy.
- Do not wait for "serious" yellowing to start spraying. As soon as
  you see the first pale yellow new leaves, spray immediately. Iron
  deficiency spreads fast under hot, alkaline conditions.

                    QUICK DIAGNOSIS REFERENCE CHART
================================================================================

WHICH LEAF IS AFFECTED? → START HERE:

OLDER (LOWER) LEAVES AFFECTED FIRST:
  → Uniform yellowing, all leaf parts yellow
      = NITROGEN deficiency
  → Purple-red or reddish-bronze discoloration on underside/stem
      = PHOSPHORUS deficiency
  → Leaf margin (edges) brown and scorched, center stays green
      = POTASSIUM deficiency
  → Interveinal chlorosis (veins stay green, between veins yellow)
      = MAGNESIUM deficiency

YOUNGER (UPPER/NEW) LEAVES AFFECTED FIRST:
  → Small leaves, bronze/rust color, short internodes, bushy plant
      = ZINC deficiency
  → Bright yellow between veins, veins clearly green, top leaves
      = IRON deficiency

GENERAL CORRECTION REMINDER:
  Soil application = slower but longer lasting correction.
  Foliar spray     = faster but temporary — repeats needed.
  Both together    = best results.
